Section One

Saudi Arabia's Mining
Transformation.

The Kingdom has embarked on one of the most ambitious mining investment programmes in the world. Understanding why that happened — and the legal framework it produced — is the starting point for everything that follows.

Saudi Arabia sits on an estimated SAR 5 trillion (approximately USD 1.3 trillion) in untapped mineral wealth. For decades that wealth went largely undeveloped, while the Kingdom's economy remained anchored to oil revenues. Vision 2030 changed the strategic calculation entirely. Mining was designated the third pillar of the national economy, alongside oil and petrochemicals, with a target contribution of $64 billion to GDP. That ambition required a legal framework capable of attracting international investment at scale.

In 2020, the Saudi Council of Ministers approved the Mining Investment Law — Royal Decree M/40 — replacing the 1997 framework that had governed the sector for over two decades. The new law restructured the licence system, strengthened investor protections, tightened environmental and community obligations, and placed the regulatory function more firmly within the Ministry of Industry and Mineral Resources (MIMR), formerly the Ministry of Petroleum and Mineral Resources. The implementing regulations followed in 2021, completing the framework.

This is not just a change in paperwork. The 2020 law creates obligations that flow downward through every tier of a mining operation — from the licence holder, to the main contractor, to the subcontractor, to the individual worker. As someone operating on a True East site, you are operating under a mining licence. That means the law's obligations apply to your actions on the ground.

Who regulates what? Two bodies carry the primary regulatory authority over mining operations in Saudi Arabia, and it is important to understand the distinction between them.

MIMR — Ministry of Industry and Mineral Resources is the lead technical and licensing regulator for the mining sector. It issues and manages exploration and mining licences, oversees compliance with mine plans, receives production and incident reports, and enforces the Mining Investment Law directly. If something goes wrong on site that triggers a statutory reporting obligation, MIMR is the primary recipient of that report. MIMR also manages the Saudi Geological Survey (SGS) and oversees the national geological data archive — which has direct implications for geological information collected under a licence.

MISA — Ministry of Investment Saudi Arabia oversees the broader investment and business environment in the Kingdom. For mining companies, MISA is relevant primarily in the context of foreign investment approvals, company registration, and the overall investment climate governance. MISA does not regulate day-to-day site operations, but its frameworks shape the legal environment within which mining licences are granted and maintained.

Beyond these two, the Ministry of Human Resources and Social Development (MHRSD) enforces labour law on all work sites, including mining. The National Centre for Environmental Compliance (NCEC) enforces environmental law under the Saudi Environmental Law. Both of these bodies have direct reach into site operations — and you will encounter their requirements throughout this guide.

What This Means for You

You do not need to know every article of the Mining Investment Law to do your job well. But you do need to understand that the law creates binding obligations at the site level — obligations that affect what you can and cannot do, what you must report, and what happens if you do not. This guide translates those obligations into practical terms for field workers, supervisors, and contractors operating under a True East licence.

Section Two

The Mining Licence
Structure.

Saudi Arabia's 2020 Mining Investment Law establishes three distinct tiers of mining licence. Each tier carries different rights, different obligations, and different consequences for the people working under it.

Understanding what type of licence authorises the work you are doing is not a technicality — it defines what activities are legally permitted on that site, what environmental and safety conditions apply, and what the consequences of a breach would be. Licence conditions are not internal company rules. They are legally binding obligations that create criminal and civil liability when violated.

True East primarily operates under Exploration Licences, with some sites advancing toward the Mining Licence stage. The exploration licence is not a lower-obligation instrument — it carries all of the environmental, safety, data, and reporting obligations of the full mining framework, applied to the specific activities permitted at the exploration stage. The fact that no ore is being extracted does not reduce your responsibilities. Every drill hole you make is subject to rehabilitation. Every geochemical sample collected is state property. Every incident must be reported.

A critical point that is frequently misunderstood on site: licence conditions are legally binding. They are not HSE guidelines or internal company standards. Violations of licence conditions are enforceable under the Mining Investment Law and may result in suspension or cancellation of the licence, financial penalties, and in serious cases, criminal liability for individuals involved in the breach. The licence is the legal instrument that gives True East the right to operate. Protecting it is everyone's responsibility.

Licence Breach Is Not an HSE Issue Only

Unauthorized activities outside a licence area, extraction of material under an exploration licence, or operating without the required environmental approvals are not merely internal disciplinary matters — they are breaches of a statutory instrument. They can result in licence cancellation, criminal charges, and site shutdown. If you are ever uncertain whether an activity is authorized under the current licence, stop and ask your supervisor before proceeding.

Section Three

Key Obligations Under
the 2020 Law.

Six categories of obligation flow directly from the Mining Investment Law to site level. These are not aspirational standards — they are legal requirements with enforcement consequences.

OBLIGATION 01

Saudi Content & Iqama Compliance

The law requires mining licence holders to comply with the Nitaqat (workforce localisation) system, which mandates minimum percentages of Saudi national employees across categories. For contractors, every worker on a True East site must hold a valid iqama (residency permit) authorising work in the relevant occupation. Field supervisors bear a direct obligation to verify contractor iqama before granting site access — not as a company policy, but as a condition that flows from the operator's licence obligations. An expired or invalid iqama on site creates exposure for the licence holder. Contractors are also responsible for ensuring their own Nitaqat compliance, but True East as the site operator carries residual liability for conditions on the site it controls.

OBLIGATION 02

Environmental Protection

Every exploration or mining licence requires an approved Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) and a binding Environmental Management Plan. A site rehabilitation bond must be lodged with MIMR before operations begin — a financial guarantee that the site will be restored regardless of what happens to the operating company. On the ground, this translates to: a current spill prevention and response plan, a waste management plan, a hazardous waste manifest system, and prohibitions on using certain chemicals (acids, solvents, specific reagents) without a prior permit from the National Centre for Environmental Compliance (NCEC). Environmental obligations are not optional and do not depend on whether something goes wrong — they are ongoing requirements throughout the licence period.

OBLIGATION 03

Community Engagement

The 2020 law formalises community notification requirements before exploration or mining activities commence in a new area. Mining licence holders must establish a formal grievance mechanism accessible to local communities, operate it throughout the licence period, and document all grievances and their resolution. Employment preference for local communities in the project area is a legal obligation, not merely good practice — it must be reflected in hiring documentation and contractor agreements. For exploration sites in remote regions, the "local community" definition extends to the nearest settled community within the reasonable impact area of the operation, including herders and seasonal residents.

OBLIGATION 04

Reporting Obligations

Reporting obligations under the 2020 law are specific and non-negotiable. Under a mining licence, quarterly production and mineral inventory reports must be submitted to MIMR. Under both exploration and mining licences, any serious incident — injury, environmental release, structural failure, or significant equipment damage — must be reported to MIMR within 24 hours. An annual safety performance report is required regardless of whether any incidents occurred. The IMS portal's near miss and incident reporting system is the internal mechanism that feeds into this statutory chain. A submission in the IMS portal starts the clock on the company's statutory reporting obligations — which is why same-day submission is not just policy but legal risk management.

OBLIGATION 05

Health & Safety Standards

The MHRSD OHS regulations apply in full to all workers on mining sites — employees, contractors, and subcontractors alike. Saudi Arabia's occupational health and safety framework references international standards including ISO 45001 as the applicable benchmark where domestic standards do not specify. For mining specifically, this means: safe equipment and PPE provision, training and competency records for all workers, health surveillance for workers in dust-heavy environments (silica, coal dust), emergency response plans, and regular equipment inspections. The hazardous nature of mining places these requirements at the highest tier of the OHS framework — minimum standards that cannot be negotiated away by contract terms between operators and contractors.

OBLIGATION 06

Geological Data and Information

This is one of the most widely misunderstood obligations on exploration sites: all geological data collected under a mining licence belongs to the Saudi state, not to the licence holder. This includes drill logs, assay results, geophysical survey data, geochemical data, maps, and interpretations. Data is held under a confidentiality obligation during the active exploration phase — the licence holder can use it but cannot publish it without MIMR authorisation. On licence expiry, all data must be submitted in full to the Saudi Geological Survey. Removing geological samples or data from the Kingdom without MIMR authorisation is a criminal offence under the Mining Investment Law. Any request by a third party to remove rock samples, core, or data from site must be escalated immediately to the HSE Manager before any material leaves the site.

Enforcement Reality

MIMR has established a regional inspection network that conducts both scheduled and unannounced site visits. Audit findings are documented and may result in notices of non-compliance, financial penalties, or licence conditions being tightened. The strongest protection against a negative audit outcome is a well-maintained site with current documentation, competent trained workers, and a functioning internal compliance system — which is exactly what the IMS portal is built to support.

Section Four

What the Law Means on Site —
Practical Implications.

Five real scenarios showing how the Mining Investment Law creates direct obligations for field teams. These are not edge cases — they are situations that arise regularly on exploration and mining sites across KSA.

Legislation written for a ministry can feel abstract when you are standing next to a drill rig at 06:00 in 38-degree heat. What follows are the practical situations where the law's obligations become concrete — and where the decisions made by site personnel directly determine whether True East remains in compliance with the conditions of its operating licence.

Scenario 1 — A Contractor Company Arrives on Site

Iqama Verification · Site Access Control · Saudi Content

A drilling contractor arrives with a crew of eight workers to begin a new borehole programme. The supervisor needs to grant site access so work can begin. This is the moment where the licence obligation becomes personal: under the terms of the True East exploration licence, and under the Nitaqat framework, site access cannot be granted to workers without valid iqama documents authorising work in the relevant occupation category.

The supervisor's obligation is to physically verify iqama documents before any individual enters the active work area. Not to delegate this to the contractor foreman. Not to assume the contractor has handled it. To personally verify. If documents are unavailable or expired, access is denied regardless of commercial pressure, programme schedules, or the contractor's assurances that "the paperwork is in process."

The reason this obligation sits with the site supervisor — rather than just the contractor's HR department — is that True East as the licence holder is the responsible party for compliance on the area covered by the licence. A MIMR inspector who finds workers on site with expired or invalid iqama will hold the licence holder accountable, in addition to the contractor. The contractor's failure becomes True East's licence risk.

The Correct Action Verify iqama documents for all arriving contractor personnel before site induction. Record the iqama number, expiry date, and occupation category in the contractor verification log. If any worker cannot produce a valid document, they wait outside the work area while the contractor resolves the issue through proper channels. No exceptions. Log all verifications in the IMS system.

Scenario 2 — A Chemical Spill Occurs

Spill Response · 24-Hour Reporting · NCEC & MIMR

During sample preparation, a container of stabilising reagent is knocked from a bench and approximately ten litres spills onto the ground near the drainage channel. The worker who caused the spill and a nearby colleague immediately contain what they can and absorb the remainder with spill kit materials. The immediate physical response is managed well. The spill is cleaned up within twenty minutes. No personnel were exposed.

Now the clock starts. The 2020 Mining Investment Law and its implementing regulations require notification of any chemical release to soil or water within 24 hours to MIMR's regional office, and separately to NCEC if the substance is on the NCEC notifiable chemicals register. This is not a reporting obligation that waits until the end of shift, until the supervisor feels it was "significant enough," or until a weekly report is compiled. The obligation begins at the moment the spill occurs.

The IMS portal near miss and incident reporting system is the first step in this chain. An incident report submitted through the portal captures the event, the substance, the quantity, the containment actions taken, and the current site status. That report is the internal record that supports the company's statutory notification — and establishes the timeline that MIMR may later inspect during a compliance audit.

The Correct Action Contain and manage the spill using the site's spill response kit. Submit an incident report in the IMS portal immediately — do not wait until end of shift. Notify the HSE Manager by phone within the hour. The HSE Manager initiates the 24-hour MIMR notification process. Preserve all spill kit materials used until the HSE Manager confirms the investigation is complete.

Scenario 3 — Ore Material Stockpiled Outside the Designated Area

Mine Plan Compliance · Licence Conditions · Report Obligation

A truck driver completing a material haul run notices that ore material is being stockpiled in a location that does not match the site map posted in the briefing area. The stockpile is approximately 200 metres from the designated ore pad, in an area that sits outside the hatched boundary on the approved mine plan map. The operation has been running this way for two shifts, apparently for logistical convenience.

This is not a minor operational variation. Under the terms of a mining licence, the approved mine plan defines the exact boundaries and conditions under which extraction, processing, and stockpiling are authorised. Material placed outside those boundaries is effectively operating outside the licence — a condition that creates direct regulatory exposure for the licence holder and the individuals who directed or permitted the activity.

The truck driver's obligation in this scenario is clear under True East's reporting framework and under the broader principle of Stop Work Authority: he has an obligation to report what he observed. His position is not to determine whether the deviation is serious or to make a judgement about the mine planner's decision — his obligation is to surface the observation through the correct channel so it can be assessed by people with the authority and information to address it.

The Correct Action The truck driver reports the observation to his direct supervisor immediately. If the supervisor does not take action, the observation should be submitted through the IMS portal as an unsafe condition report. This is not insubordination — it is the system working as designed. The HSE Manager and site management are then responsible for assessing the mine plan compliance status and taking corrective action before the next MIMR inspection cycle.

Scenario 4 — A Subcontractor Asks to Remove Rock Samples Off-Site

State Data Ownership · Geological Data · Authorisation Required

A subcontractor engaged in specialist assay work approaches the sample storage supervisor and requests to take a portion of the remaining drill core samples to their company's external laboratory in another city for independent testing. They present what appears to be a standard inter-laboratory transfer request form from their company. The supervisor is not certain whether this is a normal part of the subcontractor's scope of work.

Under Article 6 of the 2020 Mining Investment Law implementing regulations, all geological data and mineral samples collected under a mining or exploration licence are the property of the Saudi state and are held in trust by the licence holder. The licence holder — True East — is responsible for ensuring that samples and data remain within the licensed operation and are not transferred to third parties without explicit authorisation from MIMR. This applies regardless of what the subcontractor's internal paperwork says, and regardless of how routine the request may appear.

Removal of core samples from the licence area without MIMR authorisation is a violation of the Mining Investment Law. The person who permitted the removal — in this case the sample storage supervisor — may bear personal liability for that violation, in addition to the company's corporate exposure. The fact that the request came through an official-looking form is irrelevant to the legal status of the action.

The Correct Action No samples leave the site until the HSE Manager and the project geologist have confirmed MIMR authorisation exists for the transfer. The supervisor's role is to hold the request, inform the subcontractor that authorisation must be confirmed, and escalate immediately to the HSE Manager. Do not allow the samples to move pending confirmation. This is not bureaucratic obstruction — it is the correct management of state property.

Scenario 5 — An Exploration Borehole Crew Asks to Abandon a Completed Hole

Rehabilitation Obligation · Mine Closure · Permit Required

A drill crew has completed a 250-metre reverse circulation hole that has returned no material of interest. The programme is behind schedule and the crew chief asks the site supervisor whether they can simply cement the collar and move on without completing the full rehabilitation sign-off procedure — the same rehabilitation procedure required in the site's environmental management plan.

The rehabilitation obligation under the 2020 Mining Investment Law applies to every excavation made under the licence — including individual exploration boreholes, regardless of whether they intersected ore or not. The EIA that supported the licence grant included a commitment to rehabilitate all exploration disturbances. That commitment is a licence condition. Abandoning a borehole without formal rehabilitation sign-off is a breach of that condition.

The site supervisor's obligation is clear: the rehabilitation procedure must be followed. Cementing the collar alone is not sufficient if the environmental management plan specifies additional steps — such as reestablishing ground cover, marking the hole location, completing a rehabilitation record, and photographing the final state. The HSE Manager must sign off on the rehabilitation record before the crew moves to the next location. This requirement does not change based on schedule pressure, commercial convenience, or how minor the disturbance appears.

The Correct Action Follow the rehabilitation procedure as specified in the site's Environmental Management Plan. Complete the rehabilitation record form, photograph the rehabilitated hole, and obtain HSE Manager sign-off before moving the rig. If schedule pressure is causing compliance risks, that is a management issue to be raised through the proper channel — not resolved by skipping a required step on the ground.

Each of these scenarios has a common thread: a person on the ground who either knows what the correct action is and takes it, or who does not know and defaults to the path of least resistance. The 2020 Mining Investment Law does not distinguish between these two states — it holds the licence holder accountable for what happens on its sites regardless of whether the person involved understood the obligation. That is why this guide exists: so that every person operating under a True East licence has the foundational knowledge to make the right call.

Section Five

Environmental Obligations
in Detail.

Environmental compliance is not just a regulatory checkbox — it is a material operating condition. Non-compliance has direct consequences for the licence, the operation, and the individuals responsible.

The primary environmental law framework in Saudi Arabia is the Saudi Environmental Law (Royal Decree M/165, 2001), which applies across all industrial activities. The Mining Investment Law 2020 interfaces directly with this framework — the EIA requirement under the mining law references the environmental approval standards set under the Environmental Law, and the NCEC operates as the enforcement body for both instruments on mining sites.

The National Centre for Environmental Compliance (NCEC) was established specifically to enforce environmental regulations across industrial sectors. On a mining site, NCEC has authority to inspect, sample, and issue compliance notices independently of MIMR. A site can receive a MIMR inspection and pass it, and still receive an NCEC notice for an unrelated environmental issue. The two enforcement systems run in parallel, not in sequence.

Site-Level Obligation
Waste Management Plan
Every mining and exploration site must have a current, approved waste management plan that classifies all waste streams generated by the operation. This includes drill cuttings, spent fuels, chemical containers, domestic waste from the camp, worn tyres, and electronic waste. The plan must specify disposal pathways for each category — recycling, licensed landfill, specialist contractor — and must be updated whenever new waste streams are introduced (e.g., when a new chemical is added to the site). A plan that was written two years ago and has not been updated is not a compliant plan.
Site-Level Obligation
Hazardous Waste Manifest System
All hazardous waste — chemical containers, contaminated materials, spent batteries, acids, hydrocarbon-soaked absorbents — must be tracked through a manifest system. The manifest records the waste type, quantity, the person responsible for its generation, the disposal contractor who collected it, and the licensed facility it was delivered to. The manifest is the paper trail that proves lawful disposal. If hazardous waste leaves the site without a completed manifest, the licence holder has no evidence that it was disposed of legally — which is the same, in NCEC's view, as unlawful disposal. Maintain the manifest register weekly. The site office must retain all completed manifests for the period specified in the EMP.
NCEC inspectors will frequently ask to see the hazardous waste manifest register as one of their first requests on site.
Site-Level Obligation
Water Discharge Standards
Any water discharged from a mining site — whether from dewatering operations, process water, wash-down water, or storm water runoff — must meet the discharge standards set under the Saudi Environmental Law. These standards specify maximum concentrations of suspended solids, pH range, heavy metals, and hydrocarbon content. Sites operating in wadi environments have additional obligations: discharge into or near a wadi watercourse requires site-specific conditions to be included in the EMA and may require separate NCEC approval. Monitoring records must be maintained to demonstrate compliance and must be available for inspection.
Protected Zone Obligation
Heritage, Wadi, and Vegetation Exclusion Zones
Operations within 5 kilometres of a registered Hima (traditional land management area) or a designated cultural heritage site require additional EIA approval before activities commence in that buffer zone. Operations within or near wadis or protected native vegetation areas carry equivalent additional requirements. The approved mine plan will specify any protected zone boundaries. If new information about a nearby heritage or natural feature emerges during operations — including information from local community members — the HSE Manager must be notified immediately and operations in the area paused pending review by the project's environmental consultant.
Saudi Arabia is actively expanding its protected area register as part of Vision 2030's Nature initiative. Conditions that applied at licence grant may be more stringent by the next renewal.
Forward-Looking Obligation
Carbon and Emissions — the 2030 Signal
Saudi Arabia's Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) commitments under the Paris Agreement include targets for reducing domestic emissions intensity and reaching net-zero by 2060. The mining sector, as one of the major expansion areas under Vision 2030, is expected to incorporate emissions reporting into its operational framework progressively post-2030. While current licence conditions do not yet require carbon reporting for exploration-stage operations, the direction is clear: operations that establish energy and emissions monitoring now are better positioned for future licence renewal requirements. Diesel fuel consumption, generator hours, and vehicle fleet emissions are the primary measurement points for a typical exploration site.
NCEC Hotline — Available 24 Hours

The National Centre for Environmental Compliance operates a 24-hour hotline for reporting environmental incidents: 920000825. This is the number to use for any release of hazardous materials to the environment, any unexplained fish or wildlife mortality near the site, or any situation where you believe an environmental harm has occurred and immediate authority notification is required. The IMS portal near miss system is the first step for internal management — NCEC notification is a parallel external obligation for serious events.

Section Six

Worker Rights Under Saudi
Labour Law.

The Saudi Labour Law applies in full to everyone working on a True East site. Knowing your rights is not a challenge to authority — it is a legal framework designed to protect the people who do the work that drives the operation.

The Saudi Labour Law (Royal Decree M/51) is the primary framework governing employment relationships in the Kingdom. It applies to all workers — Saudi nationals and expatriates — and it applies equally to main employees and to workers engaged through subcontracting arrangements. Being hired by a subcontractor does not reduce your rights under Saudi Labour Law.

Article 121 — Right to Refuse Unsafe Work

Under Article 121 of the Saudi Labour Law, every worker has the right to refuse work that presents a serious and immediate danger to their health or safety — without penalty or threat of termination. This right exists regardless of what the worker's employment contract says, and regardless of who they are employed by. If you are asked to perform work that you believe creates an imminent risk to your life or the life of a colleague, you may stop. You should inform your supervisor of the reason. Your decision cannot be used against you in disciplinary proceedings.

This right is consistent with True East's Stop Work Authority policy and reinforces it with legal force. You do not need a policy to exercise it — it is your right under Saudi law.

Article 123 — Employer's OHS Obligations

Article 123 places a direct obligation on the employer — or the site operator — to provide workers with: safe and maintained equipment, appropriate PPE at no cost to the worker, OHS training in a language the worker understands, and health surveillance for workers exposed to occupational health risks (dust, chemicals, noise, heat). These are not good practices — they are legal obligations enforceable by MHRSD labour inspectors. A labour inspector visiting a site and finding workers without PPE, or workers who cannot demonstrate they received safety training, will issue a notice of violation against the employer. The employer in this context includes True East as the site operator, regardless of whether the worker is employed by a subcontractor.

Iqama and sponsorship — the dual-liability reality. When a worker is employed by a subcontractor who holds that worker's sponsorship under the kafala system, the sponsoring employer is responsible for the worker's iqama status, salary, and basic welfare. However, True East as the site operator carries separate obligations as the entity in control of the physical site where work is performed. This means True East cannot simply defer all worker welfare responsibility to the subcontractor. If a MHRSD inspector visits the site and finds workers living in inadequate accommodation, receiving wages below the legal minimum, or lacking the required medical insurance, the site operator can be held alongside the direct employer even if True East does not pay those workers directly.

Overtime limits for mining — Category A hazardous work. Mining is classified as a hazardous work category under Saudi OHS regulations. Workers in Category A hazardous occupations have lower overtime ceilings than general industry workers, and supervisors must not authorise overtime that would breach these limits. Extended shifts in heat, dust, or physically demanding conditions create compounding fatigue and injury risk — and the labour law reflects this. Roster design must account for the Category A designation when planning shift lengths and rotation schedules.

Medical insurance and health surveillance. All workers on a mining site — including contractor and subcontractor workers — must hold valid medical insurance as required under the Cooperative Health Insurance Act. For workers in dust-heavy environments (silica dust from drilling, coal dust, other mineral dusts), the Saudi OHS framework requires periodic health surveillance including lung function monitoring. This is not optional additional welfare — it is a statutory requirement with a defined monitoring interval.

If You Believe Your Rights Are Being Violated

If you believe your legal rights under Saudi Labour Law are being violated — whether by True East, by your direct employer, or by a site supervisor — you have the right to contact the MHRSD hotline: 19911. This applies to concerns about unpaid wages, unsafe conditions, excessive overtime, withheld documents, or any other labour law matter. You also have the right to raise the matter through True East's internal grievance mechanism without fear of retaliation.

Section Seven

Incident Reporting
Obligations.

Incident reporting under the Mining Investment Law has statutory deadlines that begin the moment an incident occurs. Knowing the timeline is not optional — missing a reporting deadline creates criminal liability under Article 38.

The IMS portal is the starting point for every incident and near miss. It is the internal system of record that supports the external statutory reporting chain. What follows is the complete reporting timeline from the moment of incident to final close-out — and the consequences of failing to meet each deadline.

T + 0 — Immediately
Fatal Accident — Immediate Notification
For any fatal accident on a mining site, the Saudi Labour Law and the Mining Investment Law both require immediate notification — meaning within the shortest practicable time — to two bodies simultaneously: the MHRSD regional labour inspector and the MIMR regional mining office. "Immediate" in this context means a phone call, not a written report. The purpose is to put regulators on notice before the site condition is disturbed. Both bodies may send inspectors to the site before the preliminary investigation report is due.
Do not disturb the scene of a fatal accident until MHRSD and MIMR have confirmed their requirements for site preservation. The scene is evidence.
T + 24 Hours
Initial Incident Notification to MIMR
Within 24 hours of any serious incident — including fatalities, serious injuries, structural failures, significant environmental releases, and major equipment damage — a formal initial incident notification form must be submitted to MIMR. This form confirms that an incident occurred, provides basic factual information (date, time, location, nature of incident, persons involved, immediate actions taken), and commits to the subsequent reporting timeline. The IMS portal incident report is the internal record that supports this submission. The 24-hour clock starts from the time of the incident, not from the end of shift, not from when management was informed.
The IMS portal submission should happen well before the 24-hour MIMR notification — ideally within the first hour. This gives the HSE Manager time to review and prepare the statutory notification before the deadline.
T + 72 Hours
Preliminary Investigation Report
Within 72 hours, a preliminary investigation report must be submitted to MIMR. This report must include: a factual description of events leading to the incident, the immediate cause identified, any initial information about contributing factors, the current status of affected personnel, the immediate corrective actions implemented, and a commitment to a full investigation. The 72-hour report does not need to be a complete root cause analysis — but it must be factually accurate and must demonstrate that a serious investigation is underway. Submitting a report that is demonstrably incomplete or misleading is itself a compliance violation.
T + 30 Days
Full Root Cause Analysis and Corrective Action Plan
The complete investigation report — including a structured root cause analysis, a full timeline, the corrective action plan with assigned responsibilities and deadlines, and any changes to procedures, equipment, or training — must be submitted to MIMR within 30 days. This report is the most important document in the reporting chain because it is what MIMR uses to assess whether the licence holder is taking the incident seriously and is capable of preventing recurrence. A thorough, honest, and well-structured 30-day report can significantly influence MIMR's response to the incident. A superficial or defensive report can trigger a formal licence review.
The 30-day report should be reviewed by the HSE Manager and signed by True East's representative before submission. It becomes part of the licence file and is retained by MIMR permanently.
Ongoing
IMS Portal — The Starting Point for Every Event
The IMS portal near miss and incident reporting system is the internal mechanism that feeds into every stage of the statutory reporting chain. A near miss report submitted today may prevent an incident that would trigger the 24-hour notification requirement tomorrow. An incident report submitted to the portal same-day gives the HSE Manager the information needed to meet the statutory deadline. The portal is not a parallel system — it is the source document for all external reporting. Every event that has any potential to generate a statutory reporting obligation must enter the IMS system before any other step is taken.
Article 38 — Criminal Liability for Failure to Report

Article 38 of the Saudi Mining Investment Law establishes criminal liability for failure to comply with the incident reporting requirements under the law. This applies to the licence holder as a corporate entity and may extend to individuals responsible for the failure — including site supervisors and HSE managers who knowingly delay or obstruct the reporting process. A delayed report is not merely a procedural oversight — it is a criminal matter under KSA law. The IMS portal submission is your protection: it establishes the timeline and demonstrates that you acted promptly on the information available to you.

Section Eight

Common Compliance Gaps —
What MIMR Inspectors Find.

These are the six most common site-level compliance issues identified during MIMR and NCEC inspections of mining and exploration operations in Saudi Arabia. They are documented patterns — not hypothetical risks. If any of these conditions exist on your site, they represent real regulatory exposure that should be addressed through the proper reporting channel.

Gap 1 — Workers Without Valid Iqama on Site

The most common labour law finding during combined MIMR/MHRSD inspections. Expired iqama, iqama with the wrong occupation category for the work being performed, and workers who present documents belonging to a different person. Iqama verification at the gate is a daily obligation, not a one-time induction check. Workers whose iqama expires during a project must be off-site until renewal is confirmed. There are no grace periods under KSA law.

Gap 2 — Chemical Use Without NCEC Permit

Many chemicals routinely used on exploration and mining sites — solvents, acids, reagents, cleaning compounds, drilling additives — are on the NCEC controlled substances register. Using them without a current NCEC permit is an environmental law violation. This applies even when the quantities seem small or the substance seems commercially common. The permit must be site-specific, current, and accessible at the site office during operations. Chemical audits during NCEC inspections will match the site's chemical register against the permits held — if a substance appears in the register without a corresponding permit, the inspector records a violation.

Gap 3 — Unregistered Waste Disposal

Spent consumables — empty chemical drums, contaminated rags, worn tyres, old batteries, fuel-soaked absorbents — being placed in general skip bins, burned in open areas, or collected by unlicensed contractors. This "informal disposal" of hazardous waste is a consistent finding in NCEC inspections and one of the most serious from an environmental liability perspective because the waste may have left the site entirely by the time the inspection occurs. The manifest system is the only protection — if a completed manifest exists, the licence holder can demonstrate lawful disposal. Without a manifest, the assumption is unlawful disposal.

Gap 4 — Excavations Not Rehabilitated

Exploration boreholes, test pits, access road cuts, and sample trenches left open or inadequately rehabilitated after the exploration phase has moved on. The most common scenario is a drill rig that moves to the next hole location leaving the previous collar inadequately sealed, without ground cover restoration, and without a rehabilitation record. Inspectors will walk completed hole lines and compare the physical condition of each location against the rehabilitation records held in the site office. A discrepancy is a direct licence condition breach. Every completed excavation must have a corresponding rehabilitation record with photographs.

Gap 5 — Unreported Incidents During Licence Audit Period

Minor incidents — a first aid injury, a small chemical splash, a near-miss with a vehicle, a piece of equipment damage — that were handled on site but not submitted to the IMS system or reported externally. These incidents are invisible to management and to regulators until a licence audit reveals them. During a MIMR licence audit, inspectors cross-reference the statutory incident reports submitted during the review period against the site's internal medical records, safety meeting minutes, and personnel statements. Incidents that appear in internal records but not in statutory reports create serious compliance problems. Reporting everything through the IMS portal from day one is the only reliable protection.

Gap 6 — Environmental Permits Not Accessible at the Site Office

The original or certified copies of the site's environmental permits — EIA approval, NCEC chemical permits, waste contractor licences, discharge approvals — must be physically accessible at the site office during operations. An inspector who asks to see a permit and is told "it's in the head office" or "we can email it to you" will record a non-compliance finding regardless of whether the permit itself is valid. Regulatory documents must be on site. The HSE Manager is responsible for maintaining a current permits file at the site office and ensuring it is updated immediately when permits are renewed or new approvals are obtained.

Section Nine

Staying Compliant —
The Site Supervisor's Checklist.

Six daily and weekly actions that keep a True East site in compliance with its licence obligations. Not a complete audit instrument — a practical operational baseline for the person responsible on the ground.

Compliance is not an event that happens when the inspector arrives. It is the cumulative result of daily discipline. The following six items represent the minimum operational standard for a compliant exploration or mining site operating under a True East licence. Each item has a direct connection to the legal obligations in the 2020 Mining Investment Law and the associated regulatory frameworks.

Contractor Verification — Check Iqama Before Site Access

Every contractor worker entering the active site area must present a valid iqama before their first access each day. Record the iqama number, expiry date, and occupation category in the contractor register. Workers whose iqama expires or whose occupation category does not match their assigned role are denied access until documentation is corrected. This is not negotiable and does not bend to schedule pressure. The register must be available for inspection at all times.

Chemical Register — Current, Approved, NCEC Permits Attached

The chemical register is a live document — it must be updated every time a new substance arrives on site and every time a substance is removed. Each chemical in the register must have a corresponding Safety Data Sheet (SDS) in the site office. For regulated substances, the NCEC permit must be physically attached to or cross-referenced in the register. A chemical register that does not match the actual substances on site is not a compliant register — it is false documentation. Conduct a weekly reconciliation between the register and the chemicals physically present in the chemical store.

Waste Manifest — Updated Weekly, Hazardous Waste Tracked

Review the waste manifest register every Friday. Every hazardous waste collection that occurred during the week must have a completed manifest entry: waste type, quantity, collection date, contractor name and licence number, and receiving facility. Manifests from closed collections should be filed in the permits folder. Any hazardous waste that left the site without a manifest is an unprotected disposal — flag it to the HSE Manager immediately so a retrospective record can be constructed where possible. A manifest that is two months behind is not a functional manifest system.

Incident Log — Any Incident, Same Day to IMS Portal

Every event that qualifies as a near miss, first-aid incident, reportable injury, environmental release, equipment damage incident, or potential licence condition breach must be entered into the IMS portal on the day it occurs — not at end of week, not during the next supervisor's shift, and not "when there's time." The IMS portal submission starts the internal clock for the HSE Manager's review. For any event that may trigger a statutory reporting obligation, the same-day submission is what allows the 24-hour external notification deadline to be met. Default to reporting. The cost of an unnecessary report is negligible. The cost of a missed reportable event is criminal liability.

Environmental Zone Map — Posted in Site Office, Current Version

The approved environmental zone map — showing the licence boundary, restricted access areas, wadi buffers, heritage site exclusion zones, designated waste storage areas, chemical store locations, spill kit locations, and emergency muster points — must be posted on the wall of the site office and briefing area at all times. This is not decoration. It is the operational reference that tells every person on site what activities are permitted where. When the environmental management plan is updated, the map must be updated to match. An outdated map is a compliance risk because it may direct workers to perform activities in areas where those activities are now restricted.

Emergency Contacts — Posted, Confirmed Current, Not Just Stored Digitally

The emergency contact list must be physically posted at the site office and at the main briefing area — not just stored in someone's phone. It must include: the MIMR regional office number, the NCEC hotline (920000825), the MHRSD labour inspector hotline (19911), the nearest hospital and emergency services, and the True East HSE Manager's direct number. Verify these numbers quarterly — regulatory office numbers change, regional offices are reorganised, and the number that was current at project start may not connect six months later. A posted emergency contact list that cannot be reached in an actual emergency is not a compliant emergency response system.

A Note on Supervisory Accountability

Under both the Mining Investment Law and the Saudi Labour Law, supervisory personnel bear personal accountability for conditions within their area of responsibility. The checklist above is not a guide for what to tell an inspector — it is a guide for how to actually run a compliant operation. The difference between the two is the difference between a site that can withstand an unannounced inspection and one that cannot. Run the site as if the inspector is arriving tomorrow, every day. That discipline is the only form of regulatory protection that actually works.

Section Ten

Key Contacts
and Resources.

The regulatory bodies, their functions, and where to reach them. Save these contacts. Post the emergency numbers in your site office.

The following contacts cover the full regulatory landscape relevant to mining operations in Saudi Arabia. Each body has a distinct function — routing a query or notification to the wrong body creates delay and may itself be recorded as a procedural non-compliance. Knowing which body handles which type of matter is part of basic operational competence for anyone in a supervisory role on a True East site.

MIMR — Ministry of Industry and Mineral Resources Lead mining regulator. Licence issuance, mine plan approval, production reporting, incident reporting, geological data. Primary statutory reporting destination for all mining incident notifications. www.mim.gov.sa
MISA — Ministry of Investment Saudi Arabia Investment environment, foreign company registration, investment licence framework. Relevant for company-level regulatory matters and investment climate governance, not day-to-day site operations. investsaudi.sa
NCEC — National Centre for Environmental Compliance Environmental law enforcement across all industrial sectors. Chemical permits, waste management compliance, discharge standards, environmental incident response. 24-hour hotline for serious environmental incidents. ncec.gov.sa — Hotline: 920000825
MHRSD — Ministry of Human Resources and Social Development Labour law enforcement, OHS inspections, iqama and Nitaqat compliance, worker welfare. Labour inspector notification required immediately for fatal accidents. Worker rights hotline available to all workers. hrsd.gov.sa — Hotline: 19911
True East HSE Manager First point of contact for all site-level incidents, near misses, compliance concerns, and regulatory notifications. Accessible via the IMS portal and by direct phone contact posted at the site office. Any incident submitted to the IMS portal automatically notifies the HSE Manager. ims-portal.site — Internal reporting starts here
Saudi Geological Survey (SGS) — under MIMR National geological data archive. All geological data collected under a licence must ultimately be submitted to SGS on licence expiry. SGS also provides access to regional geological baseline data that may support EIA and environmental baseline study preparation. sgs.org.sa
Quick Reference — When to Call Which Body

MIMR: Any incident on a licensed site. Production reporting. Mine plan changes. Geological data queries. Licence status questions.

NCEC: Environmental releases. Chemical permit queries. Waste disposal compliance. Any environmental harm or potential harm to the natural environment.

MHRSD: Worker injuries and fatalities. Labour law questions. Iqama and Nitaqat issues. Worker welfare complaints.

True East HSE Manager via IMS portal: Everything. Every event enters the IMS portal first — the HSE Manager then routes the external notifications to the correct bodies within the statutory deadlines.

The Bottom Line

Compliance is not a department.
It is everyone's daily practice.

The 2020 Mining Investment Law creates obligations that flow to every person operating under a mining licence — not just to lawyers and HSE managers. When you verify an iqama, report an incident through the portal, question an activity that seems outside the mine plan, or refuse to let samples leave the site without authorisation, you are not following a company policy. You are fulfilling a legal obligation that protects the operation, the licence, your colleagues, and yourself. The portal is where that protection starts.
