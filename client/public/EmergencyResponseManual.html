Section Two — Scenario Response

Emergency Types &
Initial Response

Know your scenario. Follow the steps in order. Do not improvise during the first 2 minutes — the steps below are proven. Every second counts.

🔥

Fire / Explosion

Rig fire · Diesel fire · Electrical fire · Camp fire · Explosion

🚨
PRIORITY ORDER: Life first. Environment second. Equipment last. Never re-enter a burning structure or vehicle. Never fight a fire that is larger than a waste bin without firefighting training and correct equipment.
  1. 1
    RAISE THE ALARM IMMEDIATELYShout "FIRE! FIRE! FIRE!" and activate the nearest fire alarm or siren. Call the radio operator. Do not wait to confirm — if you see smoke or flames, raise the alarm first.
  2. 2
    EVACUATE the areaAll non-ERT personnel evacuate to the designated Assembly Point upwind of the fire. Use the nearest safe exit. Do not use elevators. Do not stop to collect personal belongings.
  3. 3
    ISOLATE fuel and powerDriller / Operator: shut down rig, isolate all fuel lines and electrical power at isolation points. Only if safe to do so. Do not delay evacuation to perform isolation.
  4. 4
    ATTACK only if safe and trainedERT Fire Wardens only: use appropriate extinguisher (CO₂ for electrical, Dry Powder for fuel). Keep your back to the exit. If fire grows beyond incipient stage — withdraw immediately. Do not become a casualty.
  5. 5
    CALL Civil Defense — 998OSCEC calls 998. State: location, type of fire, whether anyone is trapped, access route to site. Do not end the call.
  6. 6
    ACCOUNT for all personnelAssembly Point Marshals conduct headcount. Report result to OSCEC. Identify any missing persons immediately.
  7. 7
    WAIT for All Clear from OSCECDo not return to the site until the OSCEC declares All Clear. Do not re-enter any fire-affected structure.

Extinguisher Types — Quick Guide

  • Red / Water — paper, wood, cloth only
  • Blue / Dry Powder — flammable liquids, gases
  • Black / CO₂ — electrical fires
  • Cream / Foam — flammable liquids
  • Yellow / Wet Chemical — kitchen / cooking oils

Never Do This

  • Use water on electrical fires
  • Open doors without checking for heat first
  • Go back in for personal items
  • Let smoke block your exit route
  • Fight a fire without a trained partner
🚑

Medical Emergency / Injury

Trauma · Cardiac arrest · Heat stroke · Snake bite · Unconscious person

🚨
Before approaching any casualty: Is the scene safe? Do not become a second casualty. Check for hazards before entering. Only then provide assistance.
  1. 1
    ENSURE SCENE SAFETYCheck for ongoing hazards — fire, electricity, falling objects, chemical exposure. Do not rush in without assessing. Shout to the casualty: "Are you OK?"
  2. 2
    CALL FOR HELP via radioCall on radio: "MAYDAY MAYDAY MAYDAY — Medical Emergency at [location]. One/two/multiple casualties. Send First Aider and call 997." Stay with the casualty.
  3. 3
    ASSESS ABC — Airway, Breathing, CirculationAirway: tilt head, lift chin, check for obstruction. Breathing: look, listen, feel for 10 seconds. Circulation: check carotid pulse. If no pulse and no breathing — begin CPR immediately.
  4. 4
    CALL Saudi Red Crescent — 997State: your name, location (GPS if available), nature of injury, age and sex of casualty, current condition. Request ground or air ambulance. Follow dispatcher instructions.
  5. 5
    CONTROL bleeding and stabiliseApply direct pressure to wounds. Do not remove embedded objects. Manage for shock — lay flat, elevate legs (unless spinal injury suspected), keep warm and calm.
  6. 6
    PREPARE for MedEvacIf air MedEvac: clear helicopter landing zone of loose items, mark wind direction with available material. Assign escort to accompany casualty with their ID, medical records, and incident summary.
  7. 7
    NOTIFY OSCEC and HSE ManagerIncident Commander notified. Flash Report issued within 2 hours (TE-IMS-FRM-HSE-022). Next-of-kin notification via HR Manager only — never by field personnel.
⚖

Chemical Spill / Hazardous Material Release

Fuel spill · Drilling fluid · H₂S release · Chemical storage incident

🚨
H₂S ALERT: H₂S is colourless and smells of rotten eggs. At 10 ppm — don SCBA. At 20 ppm — EVACUATE all non-ERT personnel immediately. Evacuate crosswind or upwind. Do not approach without SCBA.
  1. 1
    STOP THE SOURCE — if safeClose the valve. Right the drum. Plug the leak. Only attempt if you can do so without PPE exposure. If not safe — evacuate and report.
  2. 2
    EVACUATE and raise alarmMove all personnel upwind or crosswind of the spill. Establish an exclusion zone — minimum 50m for fuel spills, 200m for H₂S events. No ignition sources in exclusion zone.
  3. 3
    DON correct PPE before approachingChemical-resistant gloves, goggles, and if applicable full-face SCBA. Refer to the Material Safety Data Sheet (MSDS) for the substance. MSDS files are in the HSE site office.
  4. 4
    CONTAIN the spillDeploy spill kit — absorbent booms, pads, and granules. Deploy earth berm or sandbags to prevent spread to drainage, wadi, or groundwater. Never wash spill to drain with water.
  5. 5
    NOTIFY OSCEC and HSE ManagerHSE Manager notified within 1 hour. NCEC / MEWA notified for reportable environmental releases. For H₂S: Civil Defense (998) notified immediately.
  6. 6
    CLEAN UP and dispose correctlyPlace all contaminated material in labelled, sealed containers. Dispose per PROC-HSE-015 Waste and Spill Management. Do not bury, burn, or discharge to ground.
  7. 7
    DOCUMENT and reportFlash Report issued. Spill volume, substance, containment method, and disposal route recorded. Incident investigation initiated within 24 hours.
🚙

Vehicle Accident

Road collision · Desert rollover · Vehicle fire · Person struck

  1. 1
    STOP safely and secure the sceneApply handbrake. Turn off ignition. Activate hazard lights. Deploy warning triangles at least 30m from the scene in both directions if on a public road.
  2. 2
    ASSESS casualties — do not move unless dangerOnly move casualties if vehicle is on fire, in water, or in danger of rolling. Suspect spinal injury in all rollovers — immobilise the neck and spine before any movement.
  3. 3
    CALL for help — Radio, then 997 / 999Radio: "MAYDAY — Vehicle accident at [location / GPS]. [Number] casualties. Request ambulance." Call 997 for injuries. Call 999 for road accidents on public roads (legally required in KSA).
  4. 4
    PROVIDE first aidControl bleeding. Maintain airway. Treat for shock. Do not give food or water to seriously injured. Keep casualty warm and calm. Stay with them until ambulance arrives.
  5. 5
    MANAGE the sceneAssign one person to manage traffic/bystanders. Preserve tyre marks and vehicle positions for investigation. Do not move vehicles until Police have arrived (for public road accidents).
  6. 6
    NOTIFY OSCEC and HSE ManagerFlash Report issued within 2 hours. Journey Management Controller notified. Incident investigation initiated per PROC-HSE-006.
⚠
KSA legal requirement: All road traffic accidents involving injury must be reported to Police (999). Do not move injured persons or vehicles from the scene before Police arrive unless there is an ongoing safety risk.
🏗

Structural Collapse

Rig mast failure · Trench collapse · Building collapse · Dropped load

🚨
SECONDARY COLLAPSE RISK IS HIGH. Do not rush into a collapsed structure. Establish an exclusion zone immediately. Only trained rescue personnel approach.
  1. 1
    RAISE ALARM immediatelyActivate site siren and radio operator. Declare Major Emergency. All non-essential personnel evacuate the area — minimum distance: mast height + 10m, or 25m, whichever is greater.
  2. 2
    ESTABLISH exclusion zoneNo entry to collapse zone without OSCEC authorisation. Rope off or mark the exclusion boundary. Assign one person to prevent entry.
  3. 3
    ACCOUNT for personnel — identify trapped personsAssembly Point Marshals conduct headcount. Report names of any missing or trapped persons to OSCEC immediately. Do not attempt self-rescue without structural shoring equipment.
  4. 4
    CALL Civil Defense — 998Structural rescue is a Civil Defense specialty. Call immediately. State number of persons believed trapped and nature of collapse. Do not delay this call.
  5. 5
    CALL 997 for medical standbyHave First Aiders and ambulance ready at the exclusion zone boundary. Prepare for multiple casualties with potential crush injuries, spinal trauma, and dust inhalation.
  6. 6
    DO NOT RE-ENTER until clearedA competent structural engineer must certify the area safe before re-entry. This decision rests with the OSCEC and Civil Defense — never with individual workers.
🔎

Missing Person

Unaccounted at muster · Overdue vehicle · Lost in field · No comms

  1. 1
    CONFIRM the person is genuinely missingCheck their last known location, radio, and buddy. Do they have a satellite phone? Has their vehicle returned? Allow 15 minutes from scheduled return before escalating — no longer.
  2. 2
    NOTIFY OSCEC immediatelyReport the person's name, role, last known location, time last seen, physical description, and what equipment they had. OSCEC activates the Missing Person protocol.
  3. 3
    ATTEMPT radio and phone contactRadio operator broadcasts on all channels. Try satellite phone. Try the person's emergency contact. Log all attempts with time and result.
  4. 4
    INITIATE Search — trained ERT onlyDeploy two-person search teams. Assign grid areas. Maintain radio contact every 15 minutes. Do not send single searchers. Record all areas searched and result.
  5. 5
    NOTIFY Police — 999 — if not found within 1 hourIn desert environments, a missing person after 1 hour in summer heat is a life-threatening emergency. Call 999 and provide GPS coordinates of search area.
  6. 6
    CONTINUE search until resolvedDo not abandon search. Contact HSE Manager and MD. If the person is found injured, activate the Medical Emergency protocol. Document the full timeline.
⚠
KSA desert conditions — heat urgency: In summer months, a person without water in open desert can reach critical heat stroke within 2–3 hours. Treat every missing person as urgent regardless of time of day.
🌬

Severe Weather

Sandstorm (Shamal) · Flash flood · Lightning · Extreme heat >45 °C

🌓 Sandstorm / Shamal

  • On warning: secure tarps, loose items, pipe racks, ventilation inlets
  • Stop all outdoor and elevated work immediately
  • Shelter in hard-walled buildings or vehicles — close all doors and windows
  • Wear dust mask (P2 minimum) if outdoors
  • No driving until visibility > 100m and confirmed safe
  • Account for all personnel — radio check every 30 minutes

🌊 Flash Flood

  • Evacuate low ground, wadis, and drainage paths immediately — no warning given
  • Move vehicles and equipment to higher ground
  • Never attempt to cross flooded wadi — 30cm of fast water overturns a vehicle
  • Account for all field teams — check isolated crews
  • Notify OSCEC of any team unable to return
  • Await all-clear before returning to low areas

⛈ Lightning

  • Suspend all drilling operations if lightning within 10 km
  • Evacuate from high ground, masts, and metal structures
  • No open ground — shelter in hard structures or metal-roofed vehicles
  • Do not shelter under isolated trees
  • Stay away from all rig equipment until 30 minutes after last lightning

☀ Extreme Heat (>45 °C)

  • Suspend all heavy outdoor work immediately — shift to shelter
  • Increase water ration: minimum 1 litre per hour per person
  • WBGT monitoring every 2 hours — log results
  • Monitor all personnel for heat stroke signs (confusion, hot dry skin)
  • Any suspected heat stroke: call 997 immediately — this is life-threatening
  • All vehicles air-conditioned and fuelled before any movement
🚨
Heat Stroke is a KILLING emergency in KSA. Body temperature above 40°C with confusion, hot dry skin, and no sweating = CALL 997 NOW. Begin immediate cooling: ice to neck, armpits, groin. Do not delay transport to hospital.
Section Three

Evacuation Procedure

When the Major Emergency alarm sounds — three cycles of 30-second wail with 30-second silence — you move. No exceptions. No hesitation. Every second of delay costs lives.

1
Critical — Immediate

STOP what you are doing — safely

Stop your task in a safe state. If drilling: engage drill string safety if practical. If operating equipment: park safely, apply brakes, neutral gear. Do not complete your task. Life over equipment — always.

2
Critical — Immediate

ALERT others nearby

Shout the alarm to anyone in your immediate area. Confirm they have heard and are moving. Do not assume the siren reached everyone — especially in noisy areas such as the drill floor or workshop.

3
Critical — Immediate

TAKE the nearest safe evacuation route

Move to the Assembly Point via the posted evacuation route. Stay away from smoke, fire, and chemical releases. Move upwind or crosswind — never downwind of a fire or gas release. Walk briskly, do not run.

4
Mandatory

DO NOT collect personal belongings

Your phone, wallet, and gear stay behind. Personnel who delay evacuation to collect belongings endanger themselves and the entire response operation. This is a disciplinary matter if the delay is intentional.

5
Mandatory

REPORT to the Assembly Point Marshal

Go directly to your designated Assembly Point. Report to the Marshal immediately. Give your name. If you know of any person who may be missing or injured, report this to the Marshal — do not wait to be asked.

6
Process

STAY at the Assembly Point

Do not leave the Assembly Point without OSCEC authorisation. Do not use your mobile phone unless directed. No smoking at Assembly Point. Remain calm and await instructions from the OSCEC or ERT Leader.

7
Process

AWAIT the All Clear from OSCEC

Only the OSCEC (On-Site Chief Emergency Controller) can declare the All Clear — a continuous 2-minute siren. This signals that the emergency is controlled, all personnel are accounted for, and it is safe to return. Do not return before this signal.

Assembly Point &
Roll Call Procedure

🏳

Assembly Point Location Rules

  • Minimum 50 metres from any identified hazard (rig, fuel, chemicals, wellhead)
  • Located upwind or crosswind — never downwind of hazard
  • Clearly signed with permanent, lit signage (visible day and night)
  • Every site has a Primary and Secondary Assembly Point
  • Wind sock or flag installed — check before directing personnel

📋

Roll Call — Marshal Duties

  • Maintain an updated muster sheet at all times — every crew change
  • Call roll immediately on personnel arriving at Assembly Point
  • Record the name and response for every person on the site list
  • Report headcount status to OSCEC within 5 minutes of alarm
  • Report any unaccounted person by name to OSCEC immediately
  • Do not stand down until OSCEC has given written or radio authorisation
👤

Every Person Accounted For

  • Employees, contractors, subcontractors, visitors — everyone on the muster list
  • Include any personnel in vehicles or off-site but within 1 km
  • Check tool stores, ablution blocks, and remote work areas via radio
  • A person not on the muster list must be reported — do not assume
  • Any unaccounted person: OSCEC activates Missing Person protocol immediately
🚨

If a Person Is Missing

  • Report immediately to OSCEC — name, last seen location, last seen time
  • OSCEC does NOT stand down the emergency until person is found
  • ERT initiates search protocol — 2-person teams, grid search, radio check-in every 15 min
  • Call 999 Police if not found within 60 minutes
  • Never assume a missing person left voluntarily during a declared emergency
Section Four

First Aid Basics

These steps keep people alive until professional medical care arrives. Every worker should know them. These are not a substitute for formal First Aid training — they are your survival floor.

CPR — Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation

No pulse, no breathing — start immediately

  1. Check for safety — is the scene safe for you to approach?
  2. Shake the casualty's shoulders — shout "Are you OK?" No response?
  3. Call for help — radio and 997 ambulance immediately
  4. Open airway: tilt head back, lift chin, look for obstructions
  5. Check breathing: look, listen, feel for 10 seconds. No breathing?
  6. Begin chest compressions: heel of hand, centre of chest — 30 compressions, hard and fast (5–6 cm depth, 100–120/min)
  7. Give 2 rescue breaths — tilt head, pinch nose, seal lips, breathe until chest rises
  8. Continue 30:2 ratio — do not stop until AED arrives, ambulance takes over, or casualty revives
  9. Attach AED as soon as available — follow voice prompts exactly
🚨 Do not stop CPR for more than 10 seconds. Rotate compressors every 2 minutes to maintain quality. Keep going until help arrives.

Wound Management & Bleeding Control

Major cuts, lacerations, crush injuries, amputations

  1. Don gloves before touching blood or open wounds
  2. Apply direct pressure with a clean dressing or cloth — press firmly
  3. Maintain pressure continuously — do not lift to check. Add more dressing on top if blood soaks through
  4. Elevate the injured limb above the level of the heart if possible
  5. For amputations: apply tourniquet 5–8 cm above the wound. Write the time on the tourniquet. Do not remove it
  6. Do not remove impaled objects — stabilise them in place
  7. Keep the casualty warm, still, and calm
  8. Call 997 — prepare for immediate evacuation to hospital
🚨 Severe bleeding is fatal within minutes. Control bleeding before anything else. Applying a tourniquet correctly saves a life — fear of it does not.

Managing Shock

After major injury, blood loss, severe burns, or anaphylaxis

  1. Recognise shock: pale/grey clammy skin, rapid weak pulse, confusion, dizziness, rapid shallow breathing
  2. Lay the casualty flat on their back on a firm surface
  3. Elevate their legs approximately 30 cm — unless suspected spinal, chest, or head injury
  4. Keep the casualty warm: blanket over them, off cold ground
  5. Do NOT give food or water — they may need surgery
  6. Do NOT leave them alone
  7. Monitor breathing and pulse continuously
  8. Call 997 immediately — shock is life-threatening without IV fluids
🚨 Do not let a shocked casualty stand or walk. Do not give pain relief medication without medical authorisation. Transport to hospital urgently.

Heat Stroke — KSA Desert Critical Emergency

Body temp above 40°C — confusion, no sweating, collapse

  1. Recognise it: hot DRY skin (no sweating), confusion/delirium, very high body temperature, rapid pulse, possible collapse or seizure
  2. CALL 997 IMMEDIATELY — do not wait to see if they improve. Heat stroke kills.
  3. Move the casualty to shade or air-conditioning immediately
  4. Begin AGGRESSIVE cooling NOW: ice packs to neck, armpits, and groin. Wet sheets plus fan. Cold water on skin. Every minute counts
  5. Remove excess clothing and PPE
  6. Do NOT give water to drink if confused or unconscious — aspiration risk
  7. If conscious and coherent: small sips of cool water plus electrolytes
  8. Continue cooling until ambulance arrives. Do not stop
  9. Accompany casualty to hospital with incident details
🚨 Heat Exhaustion (heavy sweating, pale, weak) can be managed with rest, shade, and fluids. Heat STROKE (no sweating, confused, hot) is a KILLING emergency requiring immediate hospital care. Know the difference.
Section Five

Communication Protocol
During Emergencies

Clear communication is the backbone of emergency response. Know who to call, in what order, with what information. A confused call wastes critical minutes.

1
Immediate — T+0 minutes

Any Worker Detects Emergency

Raise alarm immediately. Shout, activate siren, or call via radio: "MAYDAY MAYDAY MAYDAY — [emergency type] at [location]." Do not investigate first. Alarm first, always.

2
Immediate — T+0 to T+2 minutes

Radio Operator Activates OSCEC

Radio Operator notifies the OSCEC (On-Site Chief Emergency Controller) immediately. OSCEC is the senior TEMC person on site (SSE / Drilling Superintendent). OSCEC takes command from this point.

3
T+2 to T+5 minutes

OSCEC Declares Emergency Level and Calls External Services

OSCEC declares Level 1, 2, or 3. For Level 2+: calls 998 (Civil Defense), 997 (Ambulance), or 999 (Police) as required. States: company name, site location, emergency type, casualties, and access route. Radio Operator logs all external calls with time.

4
T+5 to T+15 minutes

OSCEC Notifies Corporate HSE Manager

HSE Manager notified by phone. HSE Manager notifies the Managing Director if Level 2 or 3. MD activates Crisis Management Team for Level 3. All notifications logged with name, time, and content.

5
Within 2 hours

Flash Report Issued

OSCEC or HSE Manager issues Flash Report (TE-IMS-FRM-HSE-022) to: MD, HSE Manager, QA / MR. Flash Report contains: time, location, incident type, casualties, immediate actions taken, and current status. Posted to IMS portal incident register.

6
Within 24 hours of Level 2 / 3

Regulatory Notification

MHRSD notified for occupational injuries meeting reporting threshold. NCEC / MEWA notified for environmental releases. Civil Defense incident number recorded. All regulatory communications logged by HSE Manager.

Section Six

Emergency Do's & Don'ts

Good intentions in an emergency can cost lives if the wrong action is taken. These rules are absolute.

✅

Always Do This

  • Raise the alarm first — before doing anything else
  • Account for your buddy and those nearby
  • Move upwind or crosswind away from fire, smoke, or gas
  • Follow the OSCEC and ERT instructions without question
  • Give your name to the Assembly Point Marshal
  • Report any missing or injured person immediately
  • Keep communication channels clear — radios for emergencies only during response
  • Preserve the scene after the emergency for investigation
  • Cooperate fully with external emergency services
  • Complete the Flash Report honestly and on time
  • Attend all post-emergency debriefs
  • Report near misses — they are warnings, not failures
❌

Never Do This

  • Re-enter a fire-affected area — ever
  • Fight a fire that is larger than a waste bin without training and equipment
  • Use water on an electrical fire
  • Move a casualty suspected of spinal injury unless in immediate danger
  • Use mobile phones in exclusion zones (sparks and interference)
  • Leave the Assembly Point without OSCEC authorisation
  • Approach a H₂S or gas release without SCBA
  • Speak to media or external parties about an incident — direct all enquiries to the MD
  • Attempt to manage a Level 2 or 3 emergency without calling external services
  • Make social media posts about site emergencies — this is a disciplinary offence
  • Drink or eat around chemical spills or uncontrolled releases
  • Blame or discipline anyone who reported fatigue, illness, or a near miss
Section Seven

Post-Emergency
Reporting Requirements

Reporting is not paperwork for its own sake. It captures lessons that prevent the next incident. Every report is a commitment to the workforce that this will not happen again.

T+2h

Flash Report

Flash Report (TE-IMS-FRM-HSE-022) issued by OSCEC or HSE Manager within 2 hours of any serious incident. Sent to MD, HSE Manager, and QA. Contains: time, location, type, casualties, immediate actions, current status.

T+24h

Preliminary Investigation

Initial investigation commenced within 24 hours. Scene preserved, photographs taken, witnesses interviewed. Evidence secured. Factual timeline drafted. OSCEC and HSE Manager present.

T+30d

Full Investigation Report

Complete incident investigation per PROC-HSE-006 and PROC-HSE-010. Root cause analysis, contributing factors, corrective actions with owners and dates. Submitted to MD and IMS portal.

T+48h

ERT Debrief

Full debrief with all ERT members within 48 hours. What worked, what failed, what needs to change. Findings converted into Corrective Action Requests (FRM-SYS-003). Plan updated if required.

Reg.

Regulatory Notification

MHRSD: reportable injuries within legal timeframe. NCEC / MEWA: environmental releases above threshold. Civil Defense: incident reference number retained. Legal advice sought for Level 3 incidents.

🌐

IMS Portal Filing

All incident records filed in the IMS portal at ims-portal.site. Includes Flash Report, investigation report, photos, witness statements, corrective actions, and close-out evidence. Retention: 10 years minimum.

📄
IMS Portal — Incident Reporting: Submit Flash Reports, Near Miss Reports, and Hazard Observations at ims-portal.site. Log in with your site credentials. Select "Incident Register" → "New Report." All submissions are time-stamped and assigned to the HSE Manager automatically. For urgent submissions in the field: call the HSE Manager directly — the portal form can be completed on their behalf.

Section Nine — Non-Negotiable

Golden Rules

These are absolute prohibitions. Violating any Golden Rule during an emergency is a disciplinary offence and may constitute criminal negligence. They exist because past incidents — elsewhere and at home — have killed people when they were ignored.

1
Never re-enter a fire-affected building or rig without explicit authorisation from OSCEC and a structural clearance. Fire can reignite without warning. Structural failures follow fire. Smoke inhalation kills in seconds. The equipment inside is replaceable. You are not.
🔥
2
Never approach a H₂S or uncontrolled gas release without a working SCBA properly donned and a second SCBA-equipped person present. H₂S at 100 ppm causes unconsciousness in minutes. At 1000 ppm — death is immediate. Smell alone is not a reliable warning — above 200 ppm your sense of smell is paralysed.
⚖
3
Never ignore the Major Emergency alarm or fail to muster. Ever. No exceptions. A person who fails to muster makes the OSCEC assume they are missing and launches a rescue operation, diverting ERT from active response. This failure can cost lives — including the lives of the rescuers.
🚨
4
Never attempt to move a person with suspected spinal injury unless the scene is immediately life-threatening (fire, flood, or ongoing collapse). Incorrect movement of a spinal injury casualty can cause permanent paralysis or death. Only move if the risk of staying is greater than the risk of movement.
👤
5
Never use a mobile phone or create an ignition source within the exclusion zone of a fuel or chemical spill, wellhead event, or gas release. A single spark in a vapour-rich atmosphere causes an explosion. This includes vehicle engines, radios not rated EX-safe, lighters, and static from synthetic clothing.
🔋
6
Never attempt a single-person rescue in any confined space, collapsed structure, or water incident. More people have died attempting lone rescues in confined spaces than in the original incident. Two trained rescuers, correct equipment, and authorisation — or you wait.
🔎
7
Never cross a flooded wadi or drive through flood water — regardless of vehicle type, depth estimate, or time pressure. 30 cm of fast-moving water will overturn a 4x4. Flood-water depths are deceiving. The road or ground beneath is gone. People have drowned in KSA flash floods doing exactly this.
🌊
8
Never delay calling 997 / 998 / 999 because you believe you can handle it yourselves. External agencies are faster, better equipped, and trained for major emergencies. Calling them costs nothing and commits you to nothing. Not calling them and then losing control costs everything.
📞
9
Never withhold information from incident investigators or the HSE Manager — even if you believe it implicates you. True East operates a non-punitive reporting culture. Honest reporting is protected. Concealing information that could prevent future incidents is not — and is treated as a serious breach of conduct.
📄
10
Never ignore a heat stroke casualty and assume they will "walk it off." Act immediately. In KSA summer operations, heat stroke is the most likely cause of on-site fatality. Every minute without active cooling reduces survival probability. Call 997, cool aggressively, transport urgently — without hesitation.
