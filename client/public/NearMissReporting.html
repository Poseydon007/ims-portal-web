Near Miss ReportingHow & Why.

Every serious accident started as a warning that nobody heard. This guide explains why your eyes and your report might be the most important safety tool on site.

Chapter One

The accident that
almost happened.

There is a pattern to every serious accident. It never comes out of nowhere. Long before it happens, it announces itself dozens of times. We just fail to listen.

Picture this. It's early morning at the drill site. A worker is carrying a water drum from the supply truck to the rig. The ground near the rig floor has a wet patch from yesterday's fluid runoff. He steps on it, his boot slides, he grabs the truck door handle and catches himself. Nothing happened. He laughs it off, shakes his head, and keeps moving. Nobody saw it. Nobody said anything.

Two weeks later, a different worker takes the same path in the same spot. He doesn't catch himself. He goes down hard. His wrist takes the full weight of his fall. A fracture. A medical evacuation. Days of work lost. A family back home waiting for news.

Here is the brutal truth: the second worker's injury was completely preventable. Not by some expensive engineering intervention. By a single conversation — or a single form — after the first slip. If that first near miss had been reported, someone would have fixed the wet patch, put down anti-slip matting, added a drainage channel, something. The second man would have walked across dry ground and finished his shift and gone home to his family.

That first slip was not just bad luck. It was a warning. And warnings only protect us when we pass them on.

For every major accident, there are roughly 29 minor incidents — and before those, around 300 near misses that went unnoticed or unreported. Heinrich's Triangle — Industrial Safety research, adapted for field operations

Every layer feeds the one above it. Report the bottom — prevent the top.

This pyramid is called Heinrich's Triangle, and it has been confirmed over and over again across industries from construction to aviation to mining. The number of near misses before a serious accident is not a coincidence. It is a pattern. And patterns can be broken — but only if people speak up.

At True East, we operate in remote drilling environments across Saudi Arabia. Our sites are far from hospitals. Our nearest emergency services may be an hour or more away. That means we cannot afford to let warnings pass unrecorded. Every small signal matters more here than it would in the middle of a city.

When you report a near miss, you are not filling out paperwork. You are breaking the chain that leads to the top of that triangle.

Chapter Two

So what exactly
is a near miss?

People hesitate to report because they are not sure if what happened "counts." The answer is almost always yes — and here is exactly what that means.

A near miss is any event, condition, or situation that could have resulted in injury, illness, damage, or environmental harm — but didn't. Not because the risk wasn't there, but because luck stepped in. The gap between a near miss and a serious incident is often nothing more than timing, angle, or centimetres.

There are three types of situations that qualify, and you have probably experienced all three at some point.

⚠️

Something Almost Happened

An event occurred, a chain of events was set in motion — but nobody was hurt. The danger was real. The outcome was luck.

Example: A drill rod slips from a grip and falls three metres. Nobody was standing below it — this time.

Type A
🚫

Something Happened, No Injury

Contact was made with a hazard, but the injury didn't happen. Minor property damage, near-body contact, a stumble that was caught.

Example: A chemical splash reaches a worker's jacket sleeve but misses exposed skin. He was not wearing sleeve protection.

Type B
👀

An Unsafe Condition You Noticed

Nothing happened yet. But you saw something that could cause harm — a frayed rope, a missing guard, a blocked emergency exit.

Example: You notice the reversing alarm on the water truck has stopped working. Nobody hit anyone — yet.

Type C

All three types are equally worth reporting. In fact, Type C — the unsafe condition you noticed — might be the most valuable of all, because the hazard has not yet caused anything. You are ahead of the triangle. You are at the very bottom, reporting before anything has gone wrong. That is the ideal place to be.

Do not ask yourself "was this serious enough?" Ask yourself "could this have hurt someone?" If the answer is yes, it goes in the report.

Form Reference

Near miss events at True East are submitted using TE-IMS-FRM-HSE-003 — Near Miss Report Form. This is a digital form inside the IMS Portal. It takes less than five minutes to complete. Your report number is generated automatically: NM-[YEAR]-[SEQ], e.g. NM-2026-007.

Chapter Three

Why people don't
report. Honestly.

Let's not pretend this is simple. There are real reasons people stay quiet. And they are worth taking seriously — because dismissing them doesn't make them go away.

Every HSE manager knows the real number of near misses on any site is far higher than the reported number. That gap is not because nothing happens — it's because something happens to people's willingness to report it. Understanding that gap honestly is the only way to close it.

"I'll get in trouble."
Fear of blame
This is the most common reason people stay quiet. They were involved in the event, or nearby, and they worry that reporting it will point a finger at themselves. This fear is understandable — it comes from experience in other workplaces, or from watching colleagues get disciplined after honest reports.
The truth: True East's HSE policy explicitly protects workers who report in good faith. The report is about the hazard, not the person. Reporting a near miss you were involved in is a sign of maturity, not negligence.
"Everyone will think I'm soft."
Embarrassment and culture
On many sites, there is an unspoken culture of toughness. You brush things off. You don't make a fuss. Reporting a near miss can feel like admitting you were scared, or that you nearly made a mistake. It can feel like weakness in front of your crew.
The truth: The most experienced workers on any site report the most near misses — because they are the ones who know how close things can come. Reporting is a sign of expertise, not weakness. It takes professional confidence to say "I noticed something."
"Nothing happened anyway."
Minimising the event
The most natural human reaction after a near miss is relief. You were okay. Nothing bad happened. The instinct is to move on quickly and not dwell on it. Reporting it can feel like manufacturing drama over nothing.
The truth: A near miss that "didn't matter" is exactly the category that produces the most future accidents. The relief you feel is the problem — it resets your perception of the risk back to zero, while the hazard is still there waiting for the next person.
"The paperwork is too much."
Friction and complexity
On sites that rely on paper forms, reporting can genuinely feel burdensome. You have to find the form, fill it in properly, find a supervisor to hand it to, and then you never hear anything back. The effort feels disproportionate to the outcome.
The truth: The IMS Portal makes near miss reporting a five-minute process from your phone or any device. Fields are pre-populated from your login. You get a report number instantly. You are notified when action is taken. The friction has been removed intentionally.
"Nobody will do anything anyway."
Loss of faith in the system
If people have reported things before and nothing changed — the wet patch stayed wet, the broken step stayed broken — they stop believing that reporting is worth the effort. This is one of the most serious failure modes in any HSE system, because it silences the people with the most information.
The truth: Every near miss submitted through the IMS Portal enters a mandatory three-step review chain. Supervisors are notified within the hour. Corrective actions are assigned and tracked. The reporter is notified when the action is closed. The system is built to close the loop — because silence is what kills sites.
The Silence Trap

Sites with low near miss reporting rates do not have fewer near misses. They have fewer reports. The hazards still exist. The chain is still building. The only difference is that management has no visibility — until the accident happens and suddenly everyone says "I knew that spot was dangerous."

Chapter Four

Why reporting
protects you.

Let's flip the frame. Forget compliance for a moment. Forget the company. Think about what near miss reporting does specifically for the person holding the pen — or the phone.

When you report a near miss in good faith, something important happens: you shift from being a passive participant in a hazard to being the person who resolved it. You are no longer the worker who was standing near a dangerous spot. You are the worker who identified the dangerous spot and put it on record. That is a fundamentally different position.

Think about it this way. If an accident later happens at the same location, and you never reported the near miss you saw — what is your position? You knew. You said nothing. That is a difficult place to be, personally and professionally. But if you reported it, and the corrective action was assigned and tracked, and the accident still happened? You did everything right. The record shows it. Your conscience is clear.

Reporting is not just about protecting your colleagues. It is about protecting yourself — from the weight of knowing you stayed silent when you could have spoken.

Without Reporting

  • The hazard stays exactly where it is
  • The next person faces the same risk with no warning
  • If something happens, you knew and didn't act
  • You carry the knowledge of what you saw
  • The system has no data to prevent the next event

With Reporting

  • The hazard enters a tracked corrective action
  • The next person walks past a fixed problem
  • Your position is unambiguous — you acted
  • Your colleagues benefit from your awareness
  • The system learns, improves, and gets safer

There is something important about True East's culture that needs to be stated plainly: reporting a near miss does not put you in the disciplinary process. That process is for deliberate violations, for people who knowingly bypass safety controls. Honest reporting of a near miss — including one where you were involved — is protected. It is encouraged. It is what the system is designed for.

This connects directly to the Stop Work Authority. Every person at True East has the right and the responsibility to stop work when they believe a serious risk exists. Reporting a near miss is the lower-stakes version of that same authority — you are not stopping the job, you are making sure the next shift knows what you saw. That is not weakness. That is professionalism.

Stop Work Authority

Under TE-IMS-POL-HSE-001 — Stop Work Authority Policy, any worker can stop any operation they believe presents an imminent risk — without fear of reprisal. Near miss reporting is the same principle applied to non-imminent risks. See a hazard. Say something. You are protected.

Chapter Five

How to submit a report
through the IMS Portal.

It takes less than five minutes. Your information auto-fills from your login. Here is exactly what the process looks like, step by step.

The IMS Portal is available at ims-portal.site. You can access it from any phone, tablet, or computer with an internet connection. Once you're logged in with your credentials, the Near Miss Report form is two taps away from the main screen.

1
ims-portal.site → Sign In

Log in to the IMS Portal

Use your employee email and password. Your name, employee ID, department, and site are automatically attached to your session. You won't need to type them again. If you've forgotten your password, contact your supervisor or the admin.

2
Menu → Forms → HSE

Navigate to Near Miss Report Form

From the dashboard, go to Forms, select the HSE category, and find the Near Miss Report Form — form code TE-IMS-FRM-HSE-003. Your report number is generated automatically the moment you open the form. It will look like NM-2026-001.

3
Section 1 — What Happened

Describe the event

Write in plain language what you saw or what happened. No technical language required — just describe it the way you would tell a colleague. Include the date and time of the occurrence, the specific location on site, and your classification: was it something that almost happened, something that happened without injury, or an unsafe condition you noticed?

4
Section 2 — Where & When

Give location and timing details

Be as specific as possible about the location — near Rig 2, at the fuel bund, on the access road, at the sample storage area. The more precisely you can describe where it happened, the easier it is for the corrective action team to find and fix the hazard. If you can photograph it, attach the image.

5
Section 3 — Contributing Factors

What made this possible?

The form gives you a list of contributing factors to select from: environmental conditions, equipment condition, procedures not followed, inadequate lighting, time pressure, fatigue, lack of training, and more. Select all that apply. This section is the most valuable part — it tells the HSE team what conditions made the hazard possible, not just what happened.

6
Section 4 — Your Suggested Fix

Tell us how to fix it

This is optional, but it is the most powerful field in the form. You were there. You understand the site, the work, the conditions. If you have an idea — even a rough one — write it down. "Put anti-slip matting here." "Install a mirror at this blind corner." "Add a bund drain." Workers' suggestions from near miss reports become the most practical corrective actions, because they come from people who actually do the job.

7
Submit → Done

Submit and you're done

Hit Submit. Your report is time-stamped and enters the approval workflow immediately. The HSE Manager and Admin receive a notification within minutes. You receive a confirmation with your report number. Keep that number — you'll use it to follow up if needed.

Confidentiality

If you would prefer your name not to be associated with the report, indicate this in the remarks field. The HSE Manager and Admin will see your name for investigation purposes, but your identity will not be shared with others during the corrective action process. Your report number is what matters — not who filed it.

Chapter Six

What happens after
you hit submit.

Your report does not disappear into a filing cabinet. It enters a structured process. Here is exactly what that process looks like — and how you stay in the loop.

One of the biggest reasons people stop reporting is that they file a report and then hear nothing. The hazard stays unfixed. Nobody follows up. After a while, the effort feels pointless. We have built the IMS Portal specifically to prevent that experience. Every step in the workflow generates a notification. Every corrective action has an owner and a due date. Nothing disappears.

Submitted
Your report is received instantly. Report number issued. Admin and HSE Manager notified.
Step 1 Review
Supervisor reviews within 24 hours. Confirms details, adds context, may request clarification.
HSE Review
HSE Manager assesses risk level and assigns corrective action with responsible party and due date.
Action Taken
The assigned person fixes the hazard. Evidence is added to the record. Progress is tracked in the portal.
Closed
Report is closed and archived. You receive a close-out notification. The record is permanent.

The entire chain — from your submission to the final close-out — is logged in the system with timestamps, actor names, and action notes. No step can be skipped. No submission can be deleted. This is not just for compliance — it is how we ensure that your report actually results in change.

If a corrective action is overdue, the system flags it. If a report sits without a Step 1 review for more than 24 hours, there is an escalation path. The goal is that by the time you start your next shift, the hazard you reported is already being addressed.

Near miss data is also aggregated into the monthly HSE summary — TE-IMS-FRM-HSE-027 — where the number of near misses reported is tracked alongside injury rates. A high near miss rate with a low injury rate is actually a sign of a healthy safety culture. It means people are talking, hazards are being caught, and the triangle is being managed from the bottom.

Chapter Seven

Three times a
report changed everything.

These stories are fictional, but they are built on real patterns from real sites — the kind of near miss chains that play out on drilling and mining operations across KSA and around the world. Read them and ask yourself: would I have reported this?

Story 1 — The Worn Sling

Lifting Operations · Rig Site

It was a routine core lift. The sample tubes were being raised from the borehole using the winch and a wire sling assembly. As the load came up, one of the riggers noticed the sling had an unusual flex — a slight kink where the wire bent around the shackle pin. It wasn't dramatic. The lift completed without incident. But something didn't look right to him.

He almost didn't say anything. The job was done. Nobody got hurt. But during the toolbox talk the next morning, he mentioned it. His supervisor told him to put it in the portal. He submitted a near miss report — Type C, an unsafe condition — describing the sling's appearance and the location where it flexed.

The equipment inspection team pulled every sling on site within 48 hours. What they found: three slings with internal wire fatigue that was invisible from the outside. One of them — not the one the rigger had mentioned — was estimated to be within days of a failure under full load. The load it typically carried weighed over 400 kilograms, lifted above a working crew.

What happened next All three slings were replaced. A new quarterly sling inspection protocol was added to the site's maintenance register (TE-IMS-FRM-MAINT-001). The rigger who filed the report received a formal safety commendation at the next HSE committee meeting. Three people who would have been standing under that load never knew how close they came.

Story 2 — The Chemical Splash

Chemical Handling · Sample Processing Area

During sample preparation at the processing tent, a technician was pouring a stabilizing reagent from a bulk container into smaller working bottles. The container slipped on the wet workbench surface and tipped. Most of the liquid was caught, but a small amount splashed onto the bench and the floor near the drainage channel — which ran directly toward the open ground without any containment barrier.

No skin contact. No injury. The spill was cleaned up quickly. The technician filed a near miss report that afternoon: Type B, something happened without injury. His description was straightforward — the bench was wet, the container was heavy and awkward to handle one-handed, and the drain had no bund or barrier.

The HSE Manager reviewed the report and realized the same configuration existed at all four processing stations across the site. There were no secondary containment bunds around any of them. The reagents being handled included compounds that were harmful to soil if released in larger quantities.

What happened next Portable spill containment bunds were installed at all four stations within the week. A chemical handling protocol update was issued requiring two-person assistance for bulk container transfers. Four months later, a genuine container rupture occurred at one of the stations — a manufacturing defect in the seal. The entire spill was contained within the bund. Zero environmental impact. Zero skin exposure. The corrective action from a near miss four months earlier had made the difference.

Story 3 — The Reversing Truck

Vehicle Operations · Camp Access Road

The water truck reversed out of the supply bay every morning at 05:30, just as the day shift was walking from the accommodation block to the rig. The driver's rear visibility was poor — the dust accumulation on the mirrors was constant and the blind spot on the left side extended nearly eight metres. One morning, a geologist walking to the rig site stepped into that blind zone. The truck continued reversing. It stopped less than a metre from where he stood, only because another worker shouted.

No contact. No injury. But both men stood there for a moment, adrenaline still high, before the day's work continued. By the afternoon, the geologist filed a near miss report — Type A, something almost happened. He noted the truck's movement, the blind spot dimensions, and the fact that the morning walkway from accommodation converged exactly with the reversing path at the same time every day.

The near miss report triggered a vehicle risk review under TE-IMS-PROC-LOG-001. The camp layout and the truck movement schedule were both examined together — something that had never been done before because they were managed by different departments.

What happened next Three changes were implemented: extended convex mirrors were fitted to all supply vehicles, a spotter protocol was introduced for all reversing movements in the supply bay area, and the morning pedestrian walkway route was shifted to bypass the vehicle zone entirely. A yellow painted pedestrian corridor was added to the ground. None of these changes cost more than a few hundred riyals. The risk they addressed had existed from the first day the camp was operational.

In each of these three stories, the person who filed the report did not prevent just one accident. They prevented every accident that would have happened from that same hazard, on that same site, for every person who would ever work there. That is the compounding value of a single near miss report. One report, filed once, can protect hundreds of work-days across the life of an operation.

Chapter Eight

What the data
actually shows.

Near miss reporting is not a feel-good policy. It has measurable, documented impact on injury rates. Here are the numbers — and what they mean on the ground.

Studies across industrial sectors — including oil and gas, mining, and construction — consistently find the same pattern. The sites that actively encourage and act on near miss reports have dramatically fewer serious injuries. This is not a correlation that suggests they were safer to begin with. When the same sites improve their reporting culture, their injury rates follow downward.

Let those numbers sit for a moment. If your site has 300 near misses in a year — and most active drilling sites have far more — and none of them are reported, the statistical expectation is one major accident. That is not abstract. That is a person going to hospital. A family receiving a phone call. A site shutdown. An investigation. An insurance claim.

Now think about the inverse. If even 80% of those near misses are reported and acted on, you interrupt the chain. You remove hazards before they escalate. The accident that was statistically waiting for you never happens — because someone spoke up about a wet floor, a worn sling, a broken mirror, a frayed rope, a blind corner. Small things. Filed in five minutes. With enormous consequences for the people they protect.

The Monthly Injury Summary form — TE-IMS-FRM-HSE-027 — tracks near miss rates alongside Total Recordable Injury Rate (TRIR) and Lost Time Injury Rate (LTIR). Over time, as near miss reporting rates climb, those injury rates should fall. If they don't, it tells us the corrective actions aren't effective — and that is equally important information to have.

This is what an intelligent safety system looks like: it learns from near misses instead of waiting for accidents to teach it lessons the hard way.

KSA Context

Saudi Arabia's regulatory framework under MISA and the Ministry of Human Resources requires that all recordable incidents — including those in mining and exploration — be reported to the relevant authority. Near misses are not legally mandated for external reporting, but they are the data that prevents the incidents that are. A strong internal near miss culture is your first line of regulatory protection.

Chapter Nine

Five rules.
Personal commitments.

These are not company policies written in passive voice. These are direct statements — things you can say to yourself when you are standing on site and deciding whether to report something you just saw.

1
I report within 24 hours.

Not because a form says so — because the hazard does not take a day off. The longer I wait, the more likely someone else encounters it first. If I saw it today, I report it today. If I cannot access the portal on site, I tell my supervisor immediately and file the report as soon as I have access.

2
I am never penalised for reporting in good faith.

I know this because it is written into True East's policy and it is backed by Stop Work Authority principles. Reporting a near miss — even one I was involved in — is not an admission of fault. It is an act of responsibility. If anyone ever makes me feel otherwise, I can raise that with the HSE Manager directly.

3
I describe what I saw, not what I think should happen.

The most valuable near miss report is an accurate account of what actually occurred. I do not exaggerate and I do not minimise. I describe the event, the location, the conditions, and the potential outcome clearly. The HSE team will determine severity — my job is to give them accurate information to work from.

4
I follow up on my report.

My report number is my reference. If I have not received a close-out notification within a reasonable time — or if I see that the hazard I reported is still in place — I ask my supervisor about the status. The portal gives me visibility of my own submissions. I use it. My report is not the end of my involvement. It is the beginning.

5
I encourage my team to report — without pressure.

If I see a colleague hesitate after a near miss, I do not push them, but I do tell them what the process looks like and what happens to the report. I share what I know about confidentiality. I share this guide if it helps. A safety culture is built by every person on site, not by the HSE Manager alone. My behaviour sets the standard for the people around me.

These five rules are simple. They do not require expertise. They do not require training beyond what you have already received. They require only the decision — made consciously, every time — to treat the hazard you noticed as something worth passing on. That decision, repeated by every person on a site, is what the difference between a safe operation and a dangerous one actually looks like in practice.
