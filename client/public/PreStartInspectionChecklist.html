Pre-Start Equipment InspectionDaily Procedures.

Every piece of mobile plant and heavy equipment on a True East site requires a documented pre-start inspection before every shift. This guide explains exactly what to inspect, how to inspect it, and what to do when you find a defect.

Section One

Why pre-start inspections
are non-negotiable.

Equipment failure is one of the leading causes of workplace injury in the mining and resources sector. But equipment failures rarely come out of nowhere. They develop slowly, in plain sight, across many shifts — and they are stopped by a single operator who took the time to look.

Picture a bulldozer that has been in continuous operation for three weeks. Every morning, a fresh operator climbs in, keys the machine, and begins work. The hydraulic hose on the left arm actuator has been developing a small weep — a slow seepage of high-pressure fluid at a fitting that sits just below the operator's line of sight when seated. Over three weeks, the fitting corrodes, the hose blisters slightly at the joint, and the effective wall thickness decreases by nearly a third. The machine still functions. The operator still works the blade.

On day twenty-two, the hose fails under full operating pressure. The hydraulic fluid exits at several hundred psi. The operator receives third-degree burns to the left forearm and neck. He is medevaced from site. The machine is grounded for four days pending investigation.

The hose was visible during every pre-start walk-around. Nobody looked. Nobody documented. Nobody escalated. That is what this guide exists to change.

The ICAM (Incident Cause Analysis Method) principle is clear on this: equipment faults are rarely sudden. They develop progressively — a crack that propagates, a seal that degrades, a fastener that loosens over vibration cycles. The pre-start inspection is precisely the mechanism that interrupts that progression before it becomes catastrophic. It is your professional obligation, and it is the first line of protection for the people working alongside you.

Industry data across the mining sector consistently shows that sites with high pre-start inspection compliance rates have substantially lower equipment-related injury rates. The correlation is not subtle. A machine that has been inspected today is a machine whose operator has committed to the shift — and to the colleagues sharing the work area.

The 10 minutes you spend walking around your machine could be the 10 minutes that save your life. True East Mining — Equipment Operations Principle
Section Two

What equipment requires
a pre-start check?

The answer is straightforward: if it has an engine and can move people or loads — it requires a pre-start inspection, every shift, no exceptions. Here is the full equipment scope at True East sites.

The pre-start inspection requirement is not limited to large or complex machines. It applies equally to a light vehicle that carries four people across an access track and to a fifty-tonne excavator that removes overburden. The consequence of a light vehicle brake failure on a remote access road can be as severe as the consequence of a machine failure on the face. All equipment in the categories below falls under this requirement.

⚙️

Mobile Plant

Excavators, bulldozers, motor graders, motor scrapers, vibrating compactors, skid-steer loaders, backhoe loaders, articulated dump trucks, haul trucks, and surface drill rigs.

Heavy Equipment
🚗

Light Vehicles

Single-cab pickups, dual-cab crew carriers, water trucks, fuel bowsers, lube trucks, ambulance/rescue vehicles, and any vehicle used to transport personnel on site.

All Vehicles
🔨

Lifting Equipment

Mobile cranes, rough-terrain cranes, forklifts, telehandlers, man-baskets, work-platform attachments, and any sling, chain, or lifting tackle assembly in use that shift.

Lifting & Rigging
💫

Compressed Air & Hydraulic

Air compressors, pneumatic drill rigs, down-the-hole hammer units, hydraulic power packs, and any unit operating above 50 psi working pressure.

Pressure Equipment
⛽️

Support Equipment

Generator sets, camp power units, fuel storage rigs, water pumping units, sewage vehicles, and any engine-powered ancillary equipment used in daily operations.

Ancillary Plant
⚡

The Rule

Engine + ability to move people or loads = pre-start check required. If you are unsure whether a machine falls under this requirement, treat it as if it does. Ask your supervisor if uncertain.

Every Shift
Handover Between Shifts

A pre-start inspection is not relieved by a handover from the previous operator. Even if an outgoing operator tells you the machine is fine, you are responsible for confirming that with your own inspection. The defect that developed in the final hour of the last shift is yours to find. Sign the form only after your own inspection — not after someone else's word.

No Exceptions Policy

Time pressure is not a valid reason to skip a pre-start inspection. Production pressure is not a valid reason. Being told "it was fine yesterday" is not a valid reason. Under True East's HSE Management System, operating any equipment from the above categories without a completed pre-start inspection form is a breach of TE-IMS-PROC-EQP-001 and may result in disciplinary action regardless of outcome.

Section Three

The universal pre-start
sequence: WAFT.

Four letters. A complete inspection structure. Whether you are inspecting a pickup truck or a sixty-tonne excavator, the WAFT sequence gives you a consistent, repeatable method that ensures nothing gets skipped under time pressure or fatigue.

WAFT is not an acronym invented to make a training module more memorable. It is a sequence that reflects the natural physical flow of an inspection — from outside the machine to inside, from static checks to dynamic tests. It works because it is ordered the way you actually move: you walk around, you access the cab, you test the functions, and then you read the instruments. Doing it in this order means you are not climbing in and out repeatedly, and you are not missing steps because you jumped ahead.

Learn this four-letter sequence. Use it on every machine, every shift, for the rest of your career. It will eventually become as automatic as fastening your seatbelt.

W
Walk Around

Before you touch a door handle, before you put a foot on a step — walk the full perimeter of the machine. One complete circuit, close enough to see detail, from ground level to the highest accessible point without climbing.

  • Tyres or tracks — visual check all surfaces visible from ground
  • Hydraulic lines — scan for weeping, staining, or bulging
  • Structural members — look for new cracks, weld failures, impact damage
  • Ground beneath the machine — any fresh fluid on the ground?
  • Attachments — bucket, blade, ripper — pins, retaining clips, locks
  • Guards and covers — all in place, none open or missing
  • Fire suppression — nozzles clear, pressure indicator visible and green
A
Access

Now you approach to enter the cab. This phase checks everything between the ground and the operator's seat — the surfaces and structures you use to get in and out of the machine safely.

  • Steps — clear, non-slip surfaces intact, no damage or loose mounting
  • Grab rails and handrails — secure, no corrosion failure, full grip width
  • Mirrors — all mirrors present, clean, correctly adjusted or adjustable
  • Door — opens and closes fully, latch functional, seal intact
  • Window glass — no cracks in critical sightlines, no delamination
  • Seat — mounts secure, adjustment operational, headrest present
  • ROPS/FOPS — certification plate visible, not expired, structural integrity
F
Functions

Engine on. Before moving the machine, test every active safety system and control function. This is the phase where you confirm that what was built to protect you is actually working. Do not skip any item on this list — each one is there because its failure has caused an injury.

  • Horn — audible, distinctive, operational at full volume
  • Reverse alarm — test by engaging reverse; confirm alarm sounds immediately
  • All work lights — head, flood, beacon/strobe if fitted
  • Windscreen wipers — both speeds, washer reservoir has fluid
  • ROPS/FOPS — physical check for new cracks or damage since last shift
  • Seatbelt — full retraction, positive latch, webbing not frayed
  • Controls — raise/lower, swing, travel — confirm all functions free, smooth
  • Brakes — service brake functional; park brake holds on a slope
T
Talk to the Machine

Read the machine's own instruments and warning systems. A machine tells you its own condition through its gauges and warning indicators — if you know how to listen. This phase is conducted at low idle after a 2–3 minute warm-up, before any loaded or travel operation begins.

  • Oil pressure gauge — within normal operating band, no red-zone reading
  • Coolant temperature — rising to normal operating range during warm-up
  • Hydraulic oil temperature — stable, within operating band
  • Battery voltage or charge indicator — no warning state
  • Fuel level — sufficient for the planned shift; record on pre-start form
  • Check all fluid levels physically — engine oil, coolant, hydraulic, brake, transmission
  • Warning lights — no amber or red warning lights remaining after warm-up
  • Unusual sounds — knocking, squealing, rattling, exhaust irregularity

The WAFT sequence takes between eight and fifteen minutes depending on machine size and complexity. For a light vehicle, it will be closer to five minutes. For a large excavator or drill rig, allow fifteen. This time is not overhead — it is part of the shift. It is not something that happens before the shift starts and therefore competes with the start time. It is the first productive activity of your shift, and it is one of the most important.

Supervisors are expected to confirm that WAFT has been completed before machines are released to the face. The pre-start form on the IMS Portal is the verification mechanism. No form, no machine.

Section Four

Walk-around inspection —
12 checkpoints.

The Walk-Around phase of WAFT follows twelve defined checkpoints. Each checkpoint has a specific scope. Knowing what to look for at each one is what separates a meaningful inspection from a ritual walk-past.

Checkpoint 01
⚪

Tyres / Tracks

Tyres: inflation (visual), sidewall cracking, exposed cord, impact damage, uneven wear suggesting alignment or loading issues. Tracks: tension (consult OEM spec), broken or cracked track shoes, worn or missing pins and bushings, ground engagement lugs below minimum depth.

Checkpoint 02
💧

Hydraulic Systems

Inspect all accessible hose runs for weeping, staining (oil discolouration on the hose exterior), blistering, chafing against frame or structural members, and damaged or corroded fittings. Check the ground beneath each cylinder for drip marks or pooling. Any hydraulic staining on the machine's understructure warrants closer investigation before operating.

Checkpoint 03
🔧

Undercarriage

For tracked machines: inspect carrier rollers, track rollers, and idlers for excessive wear or flat spots. Check sprocket teeth for hooking or undercutting. Look for track link cracking, pin migration, or missing retaining hardware. For wheeled machines: axle seals, brake-drum backing plates, steering linkage boots and grease points.

Checkpoint 04
💋

Fluid Levels

Engine oil: dipstick or sight glass — between MIN and MAX, no abnormal colour or emulsification. Coolant: expansion tank level, no steam residue on cap threads. Hydraulic oil: sight glass or indicator — within range. Transmission fluid: per OEM spec. Brake fluid reservoir: full, no contamination. All caps and dipstick tubes fully reseated after checking.

Checkpoint 05
⛽️

Fuel System

Tank filler cap secure, sealing correctly. Inspect visible fuel lines for cracking, abrasion, or loose clamp connections. Look beneath the tank and around the fuel pump for any evidence of leakage. Record fuel level on the pre-start form — this creates a consumption baseline and flags disproportionate overnight loss indicating a possible fuel system leak.

Checkpoint 06
💥

Exhaust System

Check exhaust pipe mounting for loose brackets or cracking at weld points. On startup, observe the exhaust briefly: white smoke indicates water in the combustion system, blue smoke indicates oil burning, black smoke indicates fuel system issues or blocked air intake. Any abnormal exhaust colour that persists beyond the first two minutes of startup warrants investigation before loading the machine.

Checkpoint 07
🔍

Structural Integrity

With a torch if necessary: inspect the main structural frame, boom, stick, and chassis members for new crack initiation at high-stress zones — bucket corners, boom foot pins, cab mount brackets, and counterweight attachment points. Bent structural members, especially on the boom or stick, indicate an impact event that must be reported before the machine is returned to service.

Checkpoint 08
🛠️

Guards & Covers

All engine compartment covers must be closed and latched. Rotating parts guards — including fan belts, drive shafts, and PTO attachments — must be fully in place. Missing guards are a red-tag condition. Engine covers left open allow material ingress and create fire risk from fuel contamination on hot surfaces. Check that all underguard mounting fasteners are present and tight.

Checkpoint 09
🔂

Windscreens & Glass

Any crack or chip that lies within the operator's primary forward, side, or rearward sightline must be assessed. Cracks longer than 150mm in any sightline zone, or any crack extending from an edge of the glass, are yellow tag minimum. A crack that refracts light across the operator's critical visibility zone — particularly for reversing or working near personnel — is a red-tag condition pending replacement.

Checkpoint 10
🔥

Fire Suppression

Where fitted: confirm the system is armed (activation status indicator in armed/ready position). Inspect all nozzles for blockage, corrosion, or physical damage. Check the pressure gauge — green zone only. Confirm the manual activation pull-handle is accessible and unobstructed from the operator's seat. Any fire suppression system found unarmed, discharged, or with a gauge reading below green is a red-tag condition.

Checkpoint 11
🔔

Warning Devices

Horn: test from the cab — confirm audibility at distance. Reverse alarm: engage reverse gear and confirm the alarm activates immediately and at sufficient volume to be heard above ambient site noise. Light beacon or strobe: confirm operation on machines where fitted. Any defective warning device is safety-critical — the machine cannot move in a direction where workers may be present until the device is confirmed operational or replaced.

Checkpoint 12
🔗

Attachments

Bucket or blade: check pin retaining clips, split pins, or lock bolts — all must be present and engaged. For quick-hitch systems: confirm both locking pins are fully extended and the secondary safety latch is engaged. Rippers: check shank pins, tip wear, and lower hitch mounting. Any attachment with a missing pin retainer, unsecured lock, or worn tip beyond rated tolerance must be corrected before loading. An unsecured bucket at height is a fatal-risk condition.

These twelve checkpoints represent the minimum scope for a walk-around inspection. Experienced operators on specific machine types will develop additional checkpoint knowledge relevant to that machine's known failure modes. If you operate the same machine regularly, you will begin to develop a baseline — you will know what that machine's oil level looks like at the end of a normal shift, what its hydraulic fittings look like when clean, what a normal exhaust colour is at cold start. That baseline knowledge is valuable. Use it. When something deviates from baseline, note it and report it.

Torch Required

A pre-start inspection conducted without a torch during low-light conditions — early morning starts, night shifts, or confined inspection areas — is an incomplete inspection. Keep a torch attached to your PPE kit. The structural cracks and hydraulic weeps that matter most are often in shadow at ground level.

Section Five

Cab & controls
inspection.

Once the walk-around is complete and you have confirmed the external condition of the machine, you enter the cab. This phase focuses on operator safety systems, ergonomics, and the function test that confirms the machine is genuinely ready to work.

The cab environment is where you will spend the entire shift. Its condition directly affects your ability to operate safely and respond to hazards. A cracked mirror, a jammed armrest, a partially obscured gauge — all of these degrade your situational awareness over the course of a shift in ways that add up. The cab inspection is not a formality. It is the last verification before you start moving iron.

1
Seat & Seatbelt

Seat adjustment, condition, and restraint function

Adjust the seat to your body before starting the engine. Confirm the fore-aft and height adjustments lock positively. The seatbelt should retract smoothly, latch with a positive click, and release cleanly. Check webbing for cuts, fraying, or severe soiling that would reduce tensile strength. A seatbelt that does not latch correctly is a red-tag condition — on a machine with ROPS, the seatbelt is half the rollover protection system.

2
ROPS / FOPS

Certification plate visible and current; structure undamaged

The ROPS (Rollover Protective Structure) and FOPS (Falling Object Protective Structure) certification plate must be visible and legible in the cab. Check the recertification date — any machine with an expired ROPS/FOPS certificate cannot be operated in non-level terrain or in areas with overhead material hazard risk. Physically inspect the ROPS posts for new cracks, bends, or weld cracking at the base mounts. Any ROPS damage is an immediate red-tag condition — the system cannot absorb its rated load if it has been compromised.

3
Gauge Check at Startup

Engine oil, coolant temp, hydraulic temp, voltage — all within range

On first startup, observe all gauges during the first 60 seconds. Oil pressure must rise into the normal operating band within 10 seconds of startup — if it does not, shut down immediately. Coolant and hydraulic temperatures should begin rising from cold and stabilise within the normal range after a 3–5 minute warm-up. Battery voltage or charge indicator should show a charge state. Do not load or travel the machine until all gauges are in their normal operating bands and no warning lights remain active.

4
Function Test Sequence

Raise/lower, swing, travel, attachment — all confirmed free and smooth

In a clear area, cycle through all machine functions at slow speed before commencing productive work. For an excavator: raise boom, lower boom, crowd stick, extend stick, curl bucket, dump bucket, full swing left, full swing right, travel forward, travel in reverse. Confirm all movements are smooth, responsive, and free from hesitation, vibration, or noise. Any function that binds, hesitates, feels spongy, or produces abnormal noise requires investigation before the machine is put to work.

5
Zero Warning Lights Rule

No machine movement until all warning lights are clear

After warm-up, no amber or red warning lights should remain active on the instrument cluster. Some machines illuminate multiple warning lights at cold startup that extinguish as systems reach operating temperature — this is normal. A warning light that remains active after a 5-minute warm-up indicates a fault that the machine's own systems have detected. Identify the fault using the machine's manual or the operator code display before commencing work. Do not assume it will "go away." It will not.

The Two-Key Rule

On machines with keyswitch ignition, the pre-start form must be signed before the key is turned. If you discover a defect during the pre-start inspection and red-tag the machine, the second key (supervisor's key) is the only authorisation for restarting. This is not a bureaucratic step — it is the physical mechanism that ensures a defect is reviewed before the machine returns to service.

Section Six

The Red Tag System —
three colours, clear meaning.

True East uses a three-colour tag system for equipment status. Every operator must know what each colour means and what actions each colour triggers — for themselves and for anyone who approaches the machine after them. The tags are not suggestions. They are operational commands.

✓

Green Tag

Machine is in full operational condition. Pre-start inspection completed. Form signed and submitted. No defects found or all previously noted defects have been cleared by maintenance. Machine is released for work.

⚠

Yellow Tag

Minor defect noted. Supervisor has been informed. The defect does not constitute an immediate safety risk but must be monitored during the shift and formally reported. Machine may continue operating under close monitoring. Examples: hairline windscreen crack outside sightline, minor fluid seep not affecting level, worn tyre with cord not yet exposed.

⊗

Red Tag — OUT OF SERVICE

Machine has a defect that presents an immediate or foreseeable safety risk. Machine must NOT be operated under any circumstances until defect is repaired and signed off. Chock all wheels or tracks. Remove the key and secure in the key box. Complete the defect report in the IMS Portal. Physically attach the red tag to the ignition or controls where it cannot be missed.

The critical rule about red tags: only the maintenance supervisor may remove a red tag. Not the site manager. Not a peer operator. Not the operator who tagged it after they believe the problem has resolved. The red tag can only be removed after a formal repair has been completed, documented, and signed off by the maintenance supervisor. The removal signature is logged in the IMS Portal against the original defect report.

This rule exists because defects have a way of looking fixed before they are fixed. A leaking fitting can be tightened and appear dry for two hours before the failure propagates. A brake that feels restored may have residual air in the system. The maintenance supervisor's sign-off is not a formality — it is the verification that the person responsible for the machine's mechanical integrity has confirmed the repair is complete and the machine is safe for operation.

What Triggers a Red Tag — Non-Negotiable List

The following defects are automatic red-tag conditions, regardless of perceived severity: reverse alarm non-functional; seatbelt non-functional or webbing compromised; any evidence of brake failure or brake system sponginess; hydraulic hose blistering or sweating; tyre with visible cord; fire suppression system unarmed or discharged; ROPS/FOPS structurally damaged; any structural crack in a load-bearing member; missing or expired ROPS/FOPS certification plate.

The Excavator Boom — A Red Tag That Held

Structural Inspection · Excavator · Boom Crack

The excavator had been on the same face cut for eleven days. On the morning of day twelve, during the walk-around inspection, the operator noticed a discolouration at the lower boom foot — not a crack visible to the naked eye, but a faint grey-white mineral staining that suggested moisture was weeping from somewhere it shouldn't be. He examined it more carefully with his torch and found what appeared to be a very fine hairline fracture in the boom plate weld approximately 40mm long.

He red-tagged the machine. The site superintendent argued for a second opinion before grounding the unit — it was a critical piece of equipment for that day's programme. The operator held the tag. The maintenance team arrived and used a dye-penetrant test on the area. The crack had propagated 40% through the full thickness of the structural plate — invisible to the naked eye along its length, but a genuine structural failure in progress. The boom was operating with a fraction of its rated load capacity. Two metres of reach under a loaded bucket would likely have caused a progressive failure.

What happened The machine was taken off site for structural weld repair. It returned three days later with a certified repair and updated inspection documentation. The operator's decision to hold the red tag, under direct pressure to release the machine, prevented what the maintenance team estimated would have been a catastrophic structural failure within 20–40 operating hours.
Section Seven

Documenting your
inspection.

An inspection that is not documented did not happen. Not in any legal, operational, or HSE management sense. The IMS Portal pre-start form is a three-minute process — and it is the only mechanism that creates a permanent, verifiable record of your machine's condition.

Paper-based pre-start systems fail for a predictable set of reasons: forms get wet, lost, or illegible; they sit in a clipboard for six weeks and then go into a box that nobody ever opens; they cannot be queried to identify trends; and they cannot generate automatic notifications when defects are noted. The IMS Portal solves all of these problems. But only if the forms are actually submitted.

The pre-start form on the IMS Portal is accessible from the home screen under Active Forms. It is pre-populated with your name, employee ID, site, and the current date and shift from your login session. You will not type any of those fields. You will fill in machine ID, the 12-point checklist, any defects noted, and your signature — electronically, at the end. The entire process takes three minutes for a clean inspection and five minutes when defects need to be described.

1
ims-portal.site → Active Forms → Pre-Start Inspection

Open the pre-start form before you begin your inspection

Opening the form before you start the walk-around is good practice — it loads your session data and ensures the timestamp reflects your actual inspection time, not a submission made an hour after the inspection from the site office. The form can remain open on your phone in your pocket as you walk.

2
Machine ID field

Enter the machine ID exactly as it appears on the machine's plate

Every machine on a True East site has a machine ID plate — typically a fleet number and asset code. Enter this exactly: it is what links your form to the machine's maintenance history, defect register, and corrective action log. If you do not know the machine ID, ask your supervisor before starting the inspection. Do not leave this field blank or estimate it.

3
12-point checklist — each item Pass / Fail / N/A

Complete each checkpoint honestly

The form's checklist mirrors the 12 checkpoints from Section 4 of this guide. For each checkpoint, mark Pass (no defect found), Fail (defect found — describe below), or N/A (checkpoint not applicable to this machine type). Do not mark Pass on a checkpoint you did not physically inspect. If you are unsure whether something passes, mark it Fail and describe what you saw.

4
Defect description field

Photos are worth more than words

For any Fail entry, describe the defect in plain language and — critically — attach a photo if your phone can reach the defect location. A photo of a hydraulic hose weep, a tyre sidewall crack, or a structural mark eliminates all ambiguity for the maintenance team. It provides the before-state against which repair effectiveness is assessed. It creates a permanent visual record if the defect is later disputed. Descriptions without photos are valid — photos without descriptions are better than nothing — but both together are the standard.

5
Tag status + digital signature → Submit

Sign only after you have completed the full inspection

The digital signature on the pre-start form is a legal declaration that you have conducted the inspection and that the information provided is accurate to the best of your knowledge. Sign only after you have completed the full WAFT sequence — not before, not during. Mark the tag status: Green (no defects), Yellow (minor defect, supervisor informed), or Red (machine tagged out of service). Submit. You will receive a confirmation with a form reference number.

What the Data Creates Over Time

Every pre-start form submitted through the IMS Portal builds a machine history log. Over weeks and months, this log reveals patterns invisible to any single operator: a machine that consistently fails Checkpoint 2 (hydraulic systems) has a systemic sealing problem. A machine where tyre failures recur at Checkpoint 1 may be operating in terrain outside its class. This aggregate data drives the preventive maintenance schedule. Your individual form is both a safety check and a data point in a system that makes every machine on site safer over time.

Section Eight

Common defects and
what they tell you.

Not all defects are equal. Some are yellow-tag territory — monitor and report. Others are immediate red-tag conditions that ground the machine until a qualified technician has assessed and repaired them. Here are the eight most common defects found at pre-start, and the correct response to each.

1

Oil stains on the ground beneath the machine

Indicates a hydraulic or engine oil leak. Before moving the machine, identify the source: is it engine oil, hydraulic oil, or transmission fluid? Quantify the stain — a fresh pool larger than your hand suggests an active leak rather than historical drip. Check the corresponding fluid level before starting the engine and again after a 3-minute idle. A leak that is losing fluid at an observable rate during idle is a red-tag condition. A historical drip stain with a full fluid level and no active weep is a yellow tag — record, report, monitor.

Yellow/Red
2

Milky or cream-coloured coolant or engine oil

Water has entered the oil system — almost always indicating head gasket failure, cracked block, or cracked head. The milky emulsification occurs when coolant and oil mix under pressure. This is a red-tag condition without exception. Operating a machine with coolant contamination in the oil rapidly accelerates bearing and piston wear — the engine will fail, potentially catastrophically and without further warning. Ground the machine and submit the defect report immediately. Do not add oil or restart to "see if it clears."

Red Tag
3

Spongy or low brake pedal

Air has entered the hydraulic brake circuit, or the brake fluid level is critically low. Either condition compromises the brake's ability to generate the hydraulic pressure required to stop the machine under load. On a slope, or when reversing near personnel, a brake failure is a fatal-risk event. This is an automatic red-tag condition. The machine must not be moved — including for repositioning — until the brake system has been bled, fluid level restored, and the system pressure-tested by a qualified technician. Mark the machine Out of Service and notify maintenance immediately.

Red Tag
4

Reverse alarm not sounding

The reverse alarm is a safety-critical warning device. Its purpose is to alert ground workers in the machine's blind zones that the machine is moving toward them. A machine with a non-functional reverse alarm must not move in reverse under any circumstances — not even to reposition, not even with a spotter. Ground personnel cannot take protective action from a hazard they cannot hear coming. Red-tag the machine, complete the defect report, and notify your supervisor immediately. The alarm must be repaired and tested before the machine returns to service.

Red Tag
5

Tyre with visible cord or sidewall damage

When the rubber of a tyre has worn through to the cording material beneath — either through tread wear or sidewall impact damage — the tyre is at immediate risk of structural failure. A tyre blowout on a loaded haul truck or compactor can cause complete loss of control. Cord exposure is always a red-tag condition. Do not attempt to "baby" the machine back to the workshop. Chock the machine in place and arrange for a tyre change on site. Moving the machine on a compromised tyre risks a failure event during movement, which is far more dangerous than a stationary machine awaiting repair.

Red Tag
6

Cracked windscreen in operator sightline

A crack that passes through the operator's primary visibility zone — forward vision, reversing vision, or the zone used for spotting ground personnel — constitutes an impaired visibility condition. The severity determines the tag: a crack longer than 150mm in a critical zone is a yellow tag at minimum and a red tag if it materially obscures vision required for safe machine movement. Document with a photo, describe the crack position relative to the operator's sightline, and notify your supervisor. Do not operate the machine in conditions where the cracked area would impair a safety-critical observation.

Yellow/Red
7

Hydraulic hose sweating or blistering

A hydraulic hose that shows blistering — raised bubbles on the outer sheath — or a moist, oil-impregnated exterior weeping fluid through the outer braid is at imminent risk of high-pressure failure. Hydraulic systems on heavy equipment operate at 200–350 bar. A hose failure at these pressures is a high-velocity fluid jet that can penetrate skin and clothing, causing injection injuries that are routinely fatal or permanently debilitating. Blistering or sweating is a red-tag condition regardless of how minor it appears. The blistered outer sheath is failing — the inner layers that contain the pressure are already compromised.

Red Tag
8

ROPS plate missing, illegible, or with expired certification

Without a current, legible ROPS/FOPS certification plate, there is no documentation that the protection structure has been tested to the rated load for the machine's weight class. Certification expires because structures are modified, repaired, or age-degraded in ways that change their load capacity. An uncertified ROPS provides an unknown level of protection in a rollover event — which may be none. Any machine whose ROPS plate is missing, illegible, or expired cannot be operated in non-level terrain, tipping-risk environments, or applications where overhead object hazards exist. Red tag, notify supervisor, and contact the maintenance team for recertification.

Red Tag

The eight defects described above are the most frequently encountered at pre-start. They are not the only defects that warrant a tag — they are illustrative of the principle that a physical characteristic of the machine corresponds to a specific type of risk, and that risk has a proportionate response. Your judgment is part of the system. If you find a defect not described here and you are unsure of the correct tag level, default to Red and consult your supervisor. A machine waiting for a supervisor's assessment is always safer than a machine operating with an unassessed defect.

Section Nine

Operator responsibility
and legal standing.

The pre-start form is not just a safety record. It is a legal document. The operator's signature is a declaration. Understanding what that declaration means — and what it protects you from — is as important as understanding the inspection itself.

When you sign the pre-start inspection form, you are declaring to your employer, to the relevant regulatory authority, and potentially to a court of law that you conducted a pre-start inspection of the specified machine at the specified time, and that the information recorded on the form is accurate to the best of your knowledge and professional judgment. This is not language added to create legal complexity. It is the mechanism by which the system places responsibility accurately and protects the people who act in good faith.

That protection works in both directions. If you conduct a thorough inspection, document it honestly, and the machine later develops a fault that was not detectable at the time of your inspection, your signed form is your evidence that you did your job. If the fault was present and you documented it — and it was tagged accordingly — your form shows you acted correctly. In either case, you are protected by the record you created.

The risk is the opposite scenario. If you operate a machine that you knew, or had reasonable grounds to suspect, had a defect — and that defect contributes to an incident — you bear personal liability. Not just your employer. You. The employer has a duty to provide safe equipment; you have a duty to refuse to operate equipment you know to be unsafe. These are not competing obligations. They are complementary ones.

Your Right to Refuse

You are never obligated to operate a machine you believe to be defective, regardless of what you are told by a supervisor or site manager. This right is enshrined in Saudi Labour Law Article 121, which explicitly protects workers who refuse work they reasonably believe poses a risk to their life or health.

  • State your reason clearly: the specific defect you observed
  • Document your refusal and the defect in the portal
  • You cannot be dismissed or disciplined for a good-faith refusal
  • Escalate to the HSE Manager if pressure to comply continues

What Good Faith Looks Like

Good faith is not perfection. Good faith is thoroughness, honesty, and documentation. An operator who misses a hairline crack under a guard at zero-lux in a confined space has not failed. An operator who walked past a weeping hydraulic hose because it would slow down the start is not acting in good faith.

  • Complete all 12 checkpoints — do not skip any
  • Use a torch for areas with restricted light access
  • Document what you found, not what you wanted to find
  • Attach photos wherever a defect is visible

The Refused Machine — and What Happened Two Hours Later

Brake System · Water Truck · Refusal & Outcome

A water truck had been reported by the outgoing night operator as having a slightly soft brake pedal — the report was documented but the machine was not tagged red. It sat in the equipment park overnight. At 05:45 the following morning, the day-shift driver conducted his pre-start inspection, tested the brakes during the function test phase of WAFT, and found the pedal travel had increased significantly since the previous documented test. He classified it as a red-tag condition and submitted the defect report.

The site logistics supervisor was frustrated — the truck was needed for dust suppression on the access road before the day shift began heavy equipment movement. He told the driver the brake issue had already been "noted" in the night shift report and that the truck should be used with care. The driver refused. He cited the brake condition, his pre-start form, and his right under the HSE policy. He notified the HSE Manager directly via the portal.

The truck sat idle. At approximately 08:10, a second driver from another site crew was directed to the truck with assurance that it was "serviceable with caution." He drove it out of the park. On the first descent of the access road gradient — a 1-in-12 slope — the brake system failed to arrest the loaded truck. The vehicle ran 140 metres before the tyre friction on the soft shoulder stopped it. Nobody was injured. The near-miss report described how the truck came within 30 metres of the crusher-feed area where a crew of six was working.

Outcome and lesson The mechanical investigation confirmed that the brake master cylinder had a developing seal failure that was progressing under operational temperature. The first driver's pre-start refusal was formally commended. The supervisor who overrode the red tag received a formal improvement notice under the HSE Management System. The second driver — who did not conduct his own pre-start inspection before accepting the machine — received mandatory retraining. The machine that one driver refused to operate nearly killed six people two hours later under a different driver who was not told the full defect history.
Your Rights Under Saudi Labour Law

Saudi Labour Law Article 121 grants every worker the right to refrain from performing any task that would endanger their safety or the safety of others, provided they have reasonable grounds. This right exists independently of any company policy. True East's HSE Management System formalises and reinforces this right — but it existed before the policy was written and it exists whether you are on site or not. Use it when it is warranted. Document every use of it through the IMS Portal.
