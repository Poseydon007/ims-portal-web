Section One

The MHRSD and why it
matters on site.

The Ministry of Human Resources and Social Development is the authority behind every safety obligation at a True East site. Understanding its role is the first step toward understanding your own.

The Ministry of Human Resources and Social Development — MHRSD — is the Saudi Arabian government body responsible for labour standards and occupational safety across all industries in the Kingdom. It sets the rules for how employers must treat workers, what conditions workplaces must meet, and what happens when those conditions are not met. In the context of a mining or exploration site, it is the regulator that can walk through your gate unannounced and inspect your operation against a detailed checklist of legal obligations.

The legal framework rests on two pillars. The first is the Saudi Labour Law, enacted by Royal Decree M/51 in 2005 and amended over subsequent years. This law establishes the rights of every worker in the Kingdom — regardless of nationality or contract type — including specific provisions on occupational health and safety that apply to every employer. The second pillar consists of Ministerial Decisions that translate those broad legal principles into specific operational requirements. The most significant of these for our purposes is Ministerial Decision 4786, which sets out OHS standards for all workplaces with ten or more employees.

Mining is not a generic industry under this framework. Saudi Labour Law explicitly classifies mining as a hazardous occupation, which triggers a higher tier of obligations for employers. That classification affects shift lengths, medical examination requirements, PPE standards, and rest entitlements — all of which we will cover in the sections that follow.

Saudi OHS law does not distinguish between the nationality of the worker. It applies to every person on site. Saudi Labour Law — Principle of Equal Application

That principle matters on a remote exploration site where the workforce typically includes Saudi nationals, workers from South and Southeast Asia, and specialists from various nationalities on different contract types. The law draws no distinction. The Saudi who operates the drill rig and the expatriate geologist reviewing the core are covered by the same statutory protections, the same injury reporting obligations, the same right to a safe working environment. The employer's duty does not shrink based on who holds the iqama and who holds the work permit.

This is the framework within which True East operates. Not because compliance is a box to be ticked, but because the law sets minimum standards that are, in most respects, sensible minimum standards. Understanding them turns them from abstract regulatory text into practical tools you can use.

Regulatory Authority

MHRSD Labour Offices operate in every region of the Kingdom. The regional Labour Office for your site area has authority to conduct inspections, issue fines, and refer serious violations for legal action. Contact details for regional offices are available at hrsd.gov.sa.

Section Two

The Saudi Labour Law —
six articles you must know.

The Labour Law contains over 230 articles, but six of them define the core of occupational health and safety. Every worker on a True East site should know what these articles say.

The OHS provisions in the Saudi Labour Law are not aspirational statements. They are legally enforceable obligations backed by inspection powers, fines, and in serious cases, criminal referral. The articles below represent the floor — the minimum the law demands. True East's IMS builds on top of them, but you need to know the floor before you can appreciate the structure above it.

Article 121

Right to Refuse Unsafe Work

Every worker has the legal right to refuse any task they genuinely believe presents an imminent and serious risk to their life or health. This refusal cannot be punished. No wage deduction, no disciplinary action, no dismissal. The worker must report the hazard to their supervisor — but they cannot be forced to proceed until the risk is assessed and controlled.

Article 123

Employer's Core Safety Obligations

The employer must provide: a safe working environment, suitable tools and equipment in safe condition, personal protective equipment at no cost to the worker, mandatory health examinations, and documented safety training. Failure to provide any of these is a direct violation of the Labour Law, not merely a policy gap.

Article 125

Incident Reporting Obligation

The employer must report any occupational injury to the MHRSD Labour Office within 24 hours of the incident. This is not an internal recommendation — it is a legal requirement. Failure to report is itself a separate violation, independent of the underlying incident. The report must describe the circumstances, the nature of the injury, and the workers involved.

Article 126

Employer Bears Treatment Costs

When a worker is injured in the course of employment, the employer bears the full cost of treatment. The injured worker pays nothing. This applies from the moment of injury through to recovery or the point where treatment is concluded. Workers who have been asked to contribute to their own treatment costs after a work injury should contact the MHRSD immediately.

Article 127

Occupational Disease Coverage

Diseases caused or aggravated by work conditions are treated as work injuries for all legal purposes. This includes respiratory conditions from dust exposure, hearing loss from noise, musculoskeletal conditions from repetitive physical strain, and chemical exposure effects. The worker does not need to prove a single causative incident — a demonstrated link to working conditions is sufficient.

Article 128

Prohibition on Overloading Workers

Employers are prohibited from assigning workers to tasks that exceed their physical or technical capacity. This means assigning an untrained worker to operate machinery, requiring a worker to carry loads beyond safe manual handling limits, or assigning tasks that a worker's health examination has flagged as unsuitable are all direct violations. Job-task matching is a legal obligation, not a discretionary management practice.

These six articles are the minimum. They represent what the state decided, decades ago, was the irreducible baseline of worker protection. Every True East procedure, every IMS form, every inspection protocol exists to meet or exceed these standards. When you encounter a procedure that feels bureaucratic, it is worth knowing that behind it, somewhere, is a legal obligation that was created because someone was hurt and the system decided it should not happen again.

Article 121 in Practice

Stop Work Authority at True East — documented in TE-IMS-POL-HSE-001 — is the operational implementation of Article 121. When you invoke Stop Work Authority, you are not just following a company policy. You are exercising a statutory right. That distinction matters when you are standing on site deciding whether to stop a job.

Section Three

Employer obligations —
the full picture.

Beyond the specific articles in the Labour Law, MHRSD Ministerial Decisions set out detailed operational obligations. These are the requirements that drive True East's day-to-day HSE management.

1

Hazard Identification and Risk Assessment

A documented risk assessment is required before beginning any new task, entering a new work area, or introducing new equipment or substances. This is not optional — it is a legal requirement under MHRSD Ministerial Decision 4786. The assessment must identify hazards, evaluate the risk level for each hazard, and specify the control measures that will be applied. Generic assessments covering entire job categories are insufficient; site-specific and task-specific assessments are required. True East maintains its risk assessment register in the IMS portal, and field supervisors are responsible for conducting and documenting task-level assessments before work commences.

2

PPE Provision

The employer must provide personal protective equipment to every worker at no cost. This obligation extends beyond simply handing over equipment — the PPE provided must be appropriate for the specific hazards present, must meet relevant standards, must be properly fitted to the individual worker, and must be replaced when it is damaged or worn out. Providing substandard PPE, providing PPE that does not fit, or providing equipment that has passed its service life is a violation even if PPE is technically being provided. Workers who are issued PPE that is inadequate for the task should raise this with their supervisor and document it if the issue is not resolved. The IMS near miss reporting system can be used to flag PPE adequacy concerns.

3

Health Surveillance

For workers exposed to dust, chemicals, noise, vibration, or other occupational health hazards, mandatory medical examinations are required at three points: on hire (pre-employment), annually during employment, and on separation from employment. The pre-employment examination establishes a health baseline. Annual examinations track changes that may indicate occupational disease development. The separation examination creates a record of the worker's health status at the end of employment, which is essential for any future occupational disease claims. Results must be retained by the employer and must be made available to the worker on request. True East's health surveillance obligations are managed through the HSE department.

4

Training and Induction

Documented proof of safety training is required for every worker before they begin work. This includes a site-specific induction covering hazards, emergency procedures, PPE requirements, and reporting channels. Training records must be maintained for a minimum of five years — which means that even after a worker has left, their training history must remain accessible. Training records serve a dual purpose: they demonstrate compliance during inspections, and they provide evidence in the event of an incident investigation that the employer fulfilled their training obligation. True East tracks training records through the Education section of the IMS portal.

5

Incident Reporting and Investigation

Every injury, occupational illness, and dangerous occurrence — including near misses — must be recorded in the employer's incident register. Fatalities and serious injuries must be reported to the MHRSD Labour Office within 24 hours. The investigation of serious incidents must be documented and must identify root causes, not just proximate causes. Corrective actions from investigations must be tracked to completion. The 24-hour reporting requirement is strict — late reporting is itself a violation, separate from the underlying incident. True East's incident reporting workflow in the IMS portal is designed to meet this obligation automatically upon submission of a serious incident report.

6

Safety Committees

Workplaces with more than 100 employees are required by law to establish a joint occupational health and safety committee with equal representation from the employer and from workers. The committee must meet regularly, must review incident data, must conduct site inspections, and must have the authority to make recommendations to management. Committee minutes must be recorded and retained. For True East operations that meet the threshold, establishment and proper functioning of the OHS committee is a legal obligation, not an optional governance mechanism. Where the workforce is below 100, it remains best practice to operate a safety committee and is required under True East's IMS regardless.

The 24-Hour Clock

When a serious injury or fatality occurs on site, a 24-hour legal clock starts immediately. The MHRSD Labour Office must be notified within that window. The IMS portal incident reporting system notifies the HSE Manager and Admin the moment a serious incident report is submitted — but the formal MHRSD notification remains the employer's responsibility. Know who is responsible for making that notification before an incident happens, not after.

Section Four

MHRSD Ministerial Decision 4786 —
the operational standard.

If the Labour Law sets the principles, MD 4786 sets the practice. This Ministerial Decision is the document that makes OHS compliance concrete and auditable.

Ministerial Decision 4786 is the key document that operationalises occupational health and safety requirements for all industries in Saudi Arabia. It applies to every workplace with ten or more workers — which means every True East operation, whether a drill camp, a core processing facility, or a field base, falls squarely within its scope. Understanding MD 4786 is understanding what an MHRSD inspector will be checking against when they walk on to your site.

The documentation requirements under MD 4786 are specific and non-negotiable. Every covered workplace must maintain: an OHS policy signed by senior management, a hazard register listing all identified hazards on site, risk assessment records for each work activity involving significant hazards, training records for every worker, workplace inspection records, and a complete incident log. These are not documents that can be created after an inspector arrives — they must exist before the inspection and must show a continuous history of active safety management.

Workplace inspection obligations under MD 4786 require formal self-inspections at a minimum frequency of quarterly for operations classified as hazardous — which includes mining. These self-inspections must be documented with findings, corrective actions, and responsible parties. In addition, an annual third-party audit is required for hazardous site classifications. The audit must be conducted by a competent body independent of the employer, and the findings must be reviewed and acted upon.

Required Documentation

  • OHS Policy — signed by management
  • Hazard Register — all site hazards listed
  • Risk Assessment Records — task-level
  • Training Records — every worker
  • Inspection Records — quarterly minimum
  • Incident Log — all events, all types

Emergency Preparedness Requirements

  • Documented Emergency Response Plan
  • Evacuation routes posted at key locations
  • Trained first aiders — minimum 1 per 50 workers
  • Fire extinguisher coverage throughout site
  • Emergency drill records — documented frequency
  • Contact list for emergency services posted on site

Emergency preparedness requirements under MD 4786 are specific about ratios and coverage. The minimum of one trained first aider per fifty workers is a ratio requirement — if your site has sixty workers, you need at least two. If your qualified first aider is absent, you must ensure a replacement is available before the shift commences. Fire extinguisher coverage must be adequate for the fire risks present, which on a drilling site with fuel, solvents, and electrical equipment means significantly more than a single unit per building.

True East's IMS system supports MD 4786 compliance directly. The incident log, training records, and inspection documentation required by the regulation are all managed through the portal. When an inspector asks to see your records, the portal is where you take them.

Section Five

Specific requirements
for mining operations.

Mining's classification as a hazardous occupation under the Saudi Labour Law is not symbolic. It creates a distinct set of requirements that apply to True East operations and do not apply to lower-risk industries.

The Hazardous Occupations Annex of the Saudi Labour Law lists mining alongside other high-risk sectors including oil and gas, chemicals, and heavy construction. This classification exists because the statistical evidence, universally, shows that mining workers face injury and occupational disease rates significantly above the general workforce average. The law responds to that evidence by setting stricter standards for the sector.

Shift Limits
Maximum 6-hour exposure shifts
Where workers are continuously exposed to dust, extreme heat, or significant physical strain, the standard Saudi Labour Law shift limits are reduced. The maximum continuous exposure shift is six hours without additional premium pay, reflecting the greater physiological toll of hazardous-category work. Shifts beyond six hours in these conditions attract overtime premium obligations at rates above standard.
Rest entitlement: a mandatory paid rest break of minimum 30 minutes applies to shifts exceeding six hours in a hazardous environment. This is not at the employer's discretion — it is a statutory entitlement.
Medical Examinations
Enhanced surveillance for exposure categories
Mining workers face two specific medical examination requirements beyond the standard annual examination. Workers exposed to silica-bearing dust — which includes drilling and blasting operations in most geological environments — require an annual chest X-ray to screen for silicosis. Workers exposed to significant noise levels require annual audiometry. Both examinations must be conducted by a medical facility qualified to perform occupational health assessments, and results must be recorded and retained by the employer.
Silicosis is a progressive, irreversible lung disease caused by inhaling fine crystalline silica particles. It cannot be cured. Annual chest X-rays do not prevent silicosis — they detect it early enough to limit further exposure before the damage becomes severe. This is why the examination is mandatory, not optional.
Dust Exposure
Silica and coal dust — ACGIH TLV as minimum standard
Saudi OHS standards for respirable crystalline silica and coal dust reference the American Conference of Governmental Industrial Hygienists (ACGIH) Threshold Limit Values as the minimum acceptable exposure standard. For respirable crystalline silica, the ACGIH TLV-TWA is 0.025 mg/m³ — a standard that most uncontrolled drilling operations significantly exceed without dust suppression. Employers are obligated to measure dust levels, compare them against the TLV, and implement controls — including engineering controls first, then administrative controls, and PPE as a last line of defence — until exposures are brought below the limit.
Dust sampling must be conducted by a competent person using calibrated equipment. Results must be documented and retained. If exposures exceed the TLV, the employer must implement a corrective action plan and re-sample to verify effectiveness.
Night Shift
25% premium for hazardous occupation night work
Workers in hazardous occupations — including mining — who work night shifts are entitled to a wage premium of not less than 25% above their daytime rate. This applies to all workers on night shift, regardless of contract type or nationality. Employers who fail to apply this premium are in violation of the Labour Law, and workers who have not received the correct premium are entitled to claim the difference through the MHRSD complaints process.
Heat Stress
MHRSD seasonal outdoor work restrictions
The MHRSD issues seasonal regulations every year restricting outdoor work during the peak heat period in the Kingdom. The standard restriction prohibits outdoor work between 12:00 and 15:00 during the period from June 15 to September 15. This applies across all industries, but the hazardous occupation classification makes compliance especially critical in mining, where workers already face elevated physiological stress. The restriction applies regardless of the urgency of the work — it is a legal prohibition, not a guideline. MHRSD inspectors check compliance with this regulation specifically during the restricted period.
Exceptions exist for emergency and safety-critical operations, but these are narrow and must be documented. A general belief that the work cannot wait does not constitute an exception. True East's heat stress management procedure, linked to TE-IMS-PROC-HSE-002, sets out the site-level implementation of this requirement.
Heat Season Action

Between June 15 and September 15, all outdoor work activities at True East sites must be scheduled to avoid the 12:00–15:00 window. Shift supervisors are responsible for planning work schedules that comply with this restriction. If a task cannot be completed before noon, it waits until 15:00. There are no informal exceptions. The restriction is enforced by MHRSD with fines and, in cases of worker injury during the prohibited period, potential criminal liability for supervisors.

Section Six

Worker rights you can
exercise today.

These are not abstract legal principles waiting to be activated in some formal proceeding. They are rights you hold right now, on site, at this moment. Knowing them changes how you stand in your relationship with your employer.

Article 121

Stop Work Authority

You have the legal right under Article 121 to stop any task you believe presents a serious and imminent danger to your life or health. Document it. Report it to your supervisor immediately. You cannot be penalised, demoted, had your wages reduced, or dismissed for exercising this right in good faith. If retaliation occurs, that itself is a separate violation you can report to MHRSD.

Training Records

Request Your Training Records

Your employer must provide you with a copy of your training and induction records upon request. These records belong to you as much as they belong to the company. If you want to know what training is recorded in your name, you are entitled to see it. Request it through the IMS portal or directly from your HSE supervisor.

Health Surveillance

Access Your Medical Results

Health surveillance results from any examination conducted as part of your employment must be provided to you. If an annual chest X-ray or audiometry examination reveals an occupational condition, your employer bears the cost of all treatment — not GOSI alone, but the employer directly. You do not pay. You are not responsible for covering your own occupational disease costs.

MHRSD 19911

File a Complaint

MHRSD operates a complaints portal at musaned.com.sa and a dedicated hotline at 19911. Complaints are treated as confidential. Filing a complaint with MHRSD about a workplace safety violation is a protected act. Any retaliation by the employer against a worker who has filed a complaint is itself a separate violation carrying fines and potential reinstatement orders.

GOSI Benefits

Compensation for Work Injury

Saudi social insurance through GOSI covers work injuries from the first day of employment. If you are injured at work, you receive 75% of your wage for the first 30 days of incapacity, rising to 100% thereafter. Permanent disability compensation is calculated on scales set by GOSI based on the degree of permanent impairment. You are entitled to these benefits regardless of whether you believe the injury was "your fault."

No Deductions

No Wage Deductions for Safety Stops

If you stop work because you believe it is unsafe, and your supervisor agrees to halt the task pending assessment, you cannot have wages withheld for the time that work is paused. The time you spend waiting for a safe condition to be established is work time. This is explicitly protected under the Labour Law provisions that implement Article 121.

These rights exist in the law. They do not require you to be a Saudi national to benefit from them. They do not require a written contract that specifically mentions them. They are statutory — meaning they apply automatically by operation of law. You do not need to negotiate for them, and your employer cannot waive them on your behalf. If you ever feel that any of these rights is being denied, the path forward is clear: 19911, musaned.com.sa, or the True East HSE Manager via the IMS portal.

Section Seven

Contractor and subcontractor
obligations.

When subcontractors bring workers onto a True East site, they do not bring a separate legal framework with them. The same obligations apply — and True East shares responsibility for ensuring they are met.

The "principal contractor" rule under Saudi Labour Law places primary OHS responsibility for a site on the entity that controls and operates it. For True East operations, that is True East. When a subcontractor brings workers onto our site, we do not shed responsibility for those workers' safety by pointing to their employment contract with the subcontractor. MHRSD will hold us jointly accountable for the safety conditions those workers face while on our site. That is not just a legal technicality — it is a reflection of the reality that we control the site, its access, its infrastructure, and its work conditions.

1
Before Contract Award

Subcontractor Safety Pre-Qualification

True East must verify that any subcontractor has an active OHS program before awarding work. This pre-qualification process checks whether the subcontractor has a documented safety policy, whether their workers are trained, and whether their equipment is in inspected and certified condition. Subcontractors who cannot demonstrate basic OHS capability should not receive site access, regardless of commercial considerations. Pre-qualification records must be on file.

2
Before Work Commences

Site Safety Induction — Every Worker

True East must ensure that every contractor and subcontractor worker receives a site safety induction before beginning any work on site. This is not the subcontractor's induction — it is True East's site-specific induction covering our hazards, our emergency procedures, our reporting channels, and our PPE requirements. Even a highly experienced contractor who has worked on many sites has not worked on this site, and site-specific orientation is a legal requirement. Induction records for contractor workers must be maintained alongside those for True East employees.

3
On Entry to Site

Iqama Validity — Zero Tolerance

MHRSD inspectors check iqama validity during site audits as a standard inspection item. An expired iqama is not just an administrative gap — it is a direct violation of the Labour Law that attracts a significant fine on the sponsoring employer. The responsibility for ensuring that every worker on site has a valid iqama rests with True East as site operator. This means implementing access control procedures that check iqama expiry before allowing site entry, and maintaining a register of contractor worker iqama expiry dates. A worker with an iqama expiring within 30 days should be flagged and the subcontractor notified for renewal.

4
During Operations

Ongoing Monitoring of Contractor OHS Performance

True East's obligation does not end with induction. We must actively monitor contractor OHS performance during operations. This includes ensuring that contractor workers use the PPE they were issued, follow the safe work procedures that apply on site, and report incidents and near misses through the same IMS channels that True East employees use. A contractor worker who is injured on site is captured in our incident statistics and triggers our reporting obligations, regardless of whose payroll they are on.

5
Documentation on File

Contractor Records Requirements

The following documentation must be on file for every active subcontractor: a completed safety pre-qualification, training records for contractor workers, equipment inspection certificates for all contractor-supplied plant and tools, proof of valid iqama for every contractor worker on site, and the contractor's own incident records for work performed on True East sites. MHRSD inspectors may ask to review all of these. "The contractor has their own records" is not an acceptable response — True East must hold them independently.

Iqama Violation Risk

An expired iqama found during an MHRSD inspection results in a fine applied directly to True East as the site operator, not solely to the subcontractor. In cases involving multiple workers with expired documents, the fines compound and MHRSD can issue a recommendation for a visa ban — which affects future recruitment ability. Iqama management is not a bureaucratic convenience. It is a significant financial and operational risk.

Section Eight

MHRSD inspection —
what to expect.

An MHRSD inspector can arrive at your site announced or unannounced. Knowing exactly what they check, and how to be ready, removes anxiety from the process and keeps the focus on what matters: being genuinely compliant.

MHRSD Labour Office inspectors have broad authority during a site visit. They can access all areas of the site, interview any worker privately without a supervisor or manager present, and inspect all records. A worker who is asked to speak with an inspector privately has the right to do so, and the employer cannot lawfully prevent it. The inspector's interview with workers is often the most revealing part of the inspection — it tells them whether the safety systems that exist on paper actually exist in practice.

Arrival and Site Access

An MHRSD inspector will present credentials and state the purpose of the visit. For unannounced inspections, they may arrive during any working hour. Site management must cooperate with access requests. Inspectors may be accompanied by technical specialists for specific hazard assessments. The site's HSE Manager or their nominated representative should be identified immediately to accompany the inspection.

Document Review

Inspectors will typically begin with document review. They will ask to see the OHS policy, the hazard register, risk assessment records, training records for both employees and contractor workers, the incident log, and inspection records. Documents must be current, complete, and accessible. A missing document, an unsigned policy, or a training record that cannot be produced for a worker currently on site is a finding.

Site Walk-Through

Following document review, inspectors conduct a physical walk of the site. They are looking for PPE in actual use (not just available), safety signage posted at required locations, housekeeping and access path conditions, fire extinguisher placement and currency, first aid kit locations and completeness, and whether the physical conditions on site match the documented risk assessments. A site that looks good on paper but has PPE hanging unused and cluttered walkways will generate findings regardless of its documentation.

Worker Interviews

Inspectors may select workers to interview privately. These conversations assess whether workers actually understand site hazards, know emergency procedures, know their rights, and feel they can report concerns without fear. Workers who have received proper induction and ongoing toolbox talks will be comfortable in these conversations. Workers who have received no induction, or whose induction was perfunctory, will not be able to answer basic questions — and that reflects on the employer's compliance, not on the individual worker.

Findings and Close-Out

At the conclusion of the inspection, the inspector will present their findings — observations, non-conformances, and any immediate action items. Minor non-conformances typically result in a formal warning with a timeframe for corrective action. More serious violations may result in immediate fines. Critical violations — including fatalities, serious injuries, or systematic failure to provide basic safety — may result in a stop-work order and referral for prosecution. Findings must be addressed by the specified deadline; failure to correct a notified violation results in elevated penalties.

The best preparation for an MHRSD inspection is a thorough self-audit using the same checklist an inspector would use. Section Nine

MHRSD and the IMS Portal —
how they connect.

The IMS portal is not a standalone administrative tool. It is the primary compliance documentation system for True East's MHRSD obligations. Every report you file is a legal record.

Understanding the connection between the IMS portal and the MHRSD regulatory framework changes how you think about every form you submit. A near miss report is not just useful for internal hazard management — it is part of the incident log that MHRSD will review in any inspection. A training record created in the portal is not just an HR artefact — it is the documentary proof of compliance with the training requirements of MD 4786. The forms are not paperwork. They are your legal record.

Near Miss and Incident Reports

Every near miss or incident report submitted through the IMS portal directly feeds the MHRSD reporting obligation. For serious injuries requiring 24-hour notification, the portal immediately alerts the HSE Manager and Admin. The report record in the portal becomes the basis for any MHRSD notification. Systematic near miss reporting also demonstrates to inspectors that the site has an active safety culture, not just passive compliance.

Training Records

The Education section of the IMS portal tracks training completion for every worker. These records satisfy the MD 4786 requirement for documented proof of safety training. When an inspector asks to verify that a worker on site has received induction, the answer is in the portal. When they ask how long records have been retained, the system timestamps provide the answer. The portal is the training record system — not a supplement to paper records.

Future development of the IMS portal will extend its compliance coverage to include HSE inspection records — replacing paper-based self-audit checklists with a tracked digital workflow — and safety committee minutes, which are a required record under the Labour Law for operations above the 100-worker threshold. As these modules are deployed, the portal will represent a complete, auditable compliance record from a single system.

The practical implication for every person who uses the IMS portal is simple: your reports matter. Not because management said they do, but because they are the evidence trail that demonstrates True East is operating legally. A site that generates a consistent, honest record of near misses, incidents, training, and inspections in the portal is a site that can face an MHRSD inspection with confidence. A site where forms go unfiled, where incidents are managed informally, where training happens without records, is a site with a compliance gap — and gaps have consequences.

Every Report is a Compliance Record

When you submit a form in the IMS portal, you are creating a legal document. It carries a timestamp, your identity, and the details of an event or activity. That document can be reviewed by MHRSD inspectors, referenced in investigations, and used to demonstrate compliance. This is why accuracy matters — not just for internal management quality, but because the record you create today may be read by an inspector six months from now.
