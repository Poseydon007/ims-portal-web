Chapter 1

What is a JHA
and why it's mandatory.

A Job Hazard Analysis (JHA) — also called a Job Safety Analysis (JSA) — is a structured method to identify hazards in a task, assess the risk of each hazard, and define the controls that will protect the people doing the work.

JHA is mandatory before every high-risk task

At True East Mining, a completed and signed JHA is a condition of work — not a suggestion. No JHA means no start. This is a Stop Work Authority trigger. Any person can halt work if a valid JHA is not in place.

Think of a JHA as a five-minute safety conversation on paper. It forces the crew to ask three questions before touching the tools:

1
What are we about to do? — Break the task into clear steps.
2
What could go wrong? — Identify every hazard at each step.
3
How do we stop it from happening? — Define the controls, then work.

What triggers a mandatory JHA at True East?

Drilling operations • Working at height (>1.8 m) • Confined space entry • Hot work • Lifting operations • LOTO / maintenance on energized equipment • Chemical handling • Night operations • Any task not covered by an existing procedure • Any task where conditions have changed since the last JHA.

Chapter 2

Who fills it out
and when.

A JHA is always a team effort. The Supervisor leads the process. The field workers contribute their knowledge. Everyone signs. Everyone is responsible.

👷

Supervisor / Site Foreman

Leads and is accountable

  • Initiates the JHA before work begins — no exceptions
  • Facilitates the hazard identification discussion with the crew
  • Selects controls from the hierarchy (Eliminate → PPE)
  • Signs as the person who assessed and approved the task
  • Ensures everyone on the crew has read and signed the JHA
  • Updates the JHA if site conditions change mid-task
  • Exercises Stop Work Authority if controls fail
  • Submits completed JHA to Site HSE Manager within 24 hours
🛠️

Field Worker / Crew Member

Participates and must understand

  • Actively contributes hazards from personal experience — you know the job best
  • Asks questions if any step or control is unclear
  • Signs the JHA only when you genuinely understand it
  • Refuse to sign (and refuse to start) if controls are inadequate
  • Apply all identified controls during work
  • Stop work immediately if a new hazard appears not on the JHA
  • Report any incident or near-miss to the Supervisor right away
  • Know that exercising Stop Work Authority is your right — always

When to redo or update a JHA

A JHA is valid for one shift. If the task extends to the next shift, start a new JHA. If site conditions change (weather, equipment, crew, or scope), stop work and update the JHA before continuing. A JHA written yesterday for different conditions is not valid today.

Chapter 3

How to complete
a JHA. Step by step.

Follow these eight steps every time. Do not skip. Do not rush. A JHA done badly is worse than no JHA — it creates false confidence.

1

Complete the header information

Fill in: task name, location (GPS or grid ref), date, shift, Supervisor name, crew names, permit-to-work number (if applicable), equipment involved. Every field is mandatory. Missing information means an incomplete JHA — do not start work.

Example: Task: "Rod change at drill collar — Rig #3 / Hole TT-07." Location: "Northern Mine, Tathlith, Grid D-4." Date: 03/05/2026. Shift: Day. Permit: PTW-026-014.
2

Break the task into numbered steps

List each step of the task in the order you will perform it. Keep each step to one action. Be specific — "set up rig" is too vague. "Position the rod handler arm over the drill collar" is correct. Aim for 5–15 steps for most tasks. More than 20 means the task is too complex and should be broken into separate JHAs.

Example steps for rod change: 1. Signal driller to stop rotation and raise bit to safe height. 2. Apply LOTO on hydraulic and rotation power. 3. Position rod handler arm. 4. Break joint at drill collar. 5. Extract rod section to laydown area...
3

Identify hazards at each step

For every step, ask: what could injure someone here? Consider all hazard categories (see Chapter 5). Write the specific hazard, not just the type. Use the question: "What could go wrong, and what would the injury be?" Multiple hazards per step are common — list them all. Do not skip a step because it "seems safe".

Example: Step 4 (Break joint) — Hazard 1: "Unexpected rotation of drill string if LOTO is incomplete — entanglement / crush injury." Hazard 2: "Release of compressed air from pneumatic lines — high-pressure injection injury." Hazard 3: "Drop of drill rod section — foot/leg crush."
4

Rate each hazard using the risk matrix

Assign a risk rating to each hazard using the matrix in Chapter 4. Rate the inherent risk first (before controls). This shows why controls are needed. Then rate the residual risk (after controls). Residual risk must be Low or Medium before work can proceed. Any residual High or Critical risk requires sign-off from the Site HSE Manager and Site Supervisor.

Example: Unexpected rotation → Likelihood 4 (Likely) × Consequence 5 (Fatality) = CRITICAL. After LOTO verification: Likelihood 1 (Rare) × Consequence 5 = LOW.
5

Define controls using the hierarchy

For each hazard, list the controls you will apply. Always start from the top of the hierarchy: can you eliminate the hazard? If not, can you substitute? Work down. You will often end up with multiple controls for a single hazard. PPE should never be the only control for a High or Critical hazard — it is always a last layer, not a first.

Example controls for unexpected rotation: Engineering: LOTO applied to hydraulic and rotation power (TE-IMS-SOP-HSE-003). Verify zero energy. Admin: visual check by Supervisor before approach. PPE: FR coveralls, hard hat, safety boots. All three layers active.
6

Assign responsibility for each control

Write a person's name or role next to every control. "LOTO applied by: Isolation Officer (Ahmed Al-Rashidi)." If no one owns a control, it will not be done. This is how incidents happen. Ownership is not optional.

Rule: If you write a control and leave the "Responsible" column blank, the JHA is incomplete. Do not submit it. Do not start work.
7

Brief the whole crew — everyone reads and signs

The Supervisor walks through every step and every control verbally. Crew members ask questions. No one signs until they understand. Signing is a personal commitment: "I understand what I'm doing, I understand the hazards, and I understand how I am protected." If someone does not understand the language, provide a verbal translation before they sign.

Important: A crew member who does not understand the JHA but signs anyway creates a liability for the company and for themselves. If you are the Supervisor, do not accept a blank signature — confirm understanding with a direct question.
8

Monitor, adapt, and close out

During the task, the Supervisor checks that controls are being applied. If a new hazard is identified, stop work, update the JHA, re-brief, and restart. When the task is complete, the Supervisor signs the close-out section and submits the JHA to the Site HSE Manager within 24 hours. Records are retained in the IMS for a minimum of 5 years.

Remember: A JHA is a living document during the task. Crossing out and initialing a change in the field is better than ignoring a new hazard. If the change is significant, get a new form.

Chapter 5

Common hazard categories
on drilling sites.

Use this list as a prompt when you are identifying hazards. Run through each category for every task step. If it does not apply, move on. If it might apply, list it.

⚙️

Mechanical

Moving parts, rotation, pressure, crush points.

  • Drill string rotation — entanglement, degloving
  • Rod handler arm — pinch, crush
  • High-pressure hydraulic lines — injection injuries
  • Compressed air — pneumatic release, projectiles
  • Dropped rods or casing — impact, crush
  • Mast collapse — structural failure
⚡

Electrical

Shock, arc flash, fire from electrical faults.

  • Generator connections — electric shock
  • Damaged cables — earth fault, fire
  • Arc flash from switchgear — burns, blindness
  • Working near live circuits — electrocution
  • Inadequate LOTO on electrical isolation — re-energization
  • Wet conditions + electrical equipment
⚗️

Chemical

Toxic, flammable, corrosive, or reactive substances.

  • Drilling fluid additives — skin / eye contact
  • Diesel fuel spill — fire, environmental
  • H₂S gas — toxic inhalation
  • Dust (silica) — long-term lung disease
  • Battery acid — chemical burns
  • Solvents — inhalation, fire
⚠️

Working at Height

Any work above 1.8 m from a stable surface.

  • Mast work — fall from height
  • Working on rig platform — slip, trip, fall
  • Dropped tools from height — struck-by hazard below
  • Inadequate access ladder — fall during climb
  • Unsecured handrails or grating — sudden collapse
  • Heat + height — dizziness, loss of grip
💪

Manual Handling

Lifting, carrying, pushing, pulling heavy items.

  • Drill rod handling — back strain, hernia
  • Core box stacking — shoulder / wrist injury
  • Lifting engine components — crush, muscle tear
  • Repetitive bending / twisting — cumulative injury
  • Sand bags for site prep — strain in heat
  • No team lift plan for heavy items
🌡️

Environmental

Heat, dust, weather, terrain, wildlife.

  • Extreme heat (KSA summer, WBGT >32°C) — heat stroke
  • Silica dust — silicosis over time
  • Loose desert terrain — vehicle rollover
  • Night operations — reduced visibility, fatigue
  • Sandstorms — reduced visibility, inhalation
  • Venomous animals (snakes, scorpions) on site

Additional categories to consider

Ergonomic • Fire and explosion • Confined space • Radiation (sun / radon) • Noise and vibration • Biological (waste water, mould in core samples) • Psychosocial (fatigue, stress, heat stress affecting judgment) • Gravity (suspended loads, dropped objects)

Chapter 6

The Hierarchy of Controls.

When you choose controls for a hazard, always start at the top and work down. Higher controls are more effective because they do not rely on human behavior. PPE is the last line of defense — never the only one.

1

Eliminate Most Effective

Remove the hazard completely. If there is no hazard, there is no risk. This is the only 100% reliable control. Ask: do we actually need to do this step? Can we redesign the task to remove the hazard?

▲
2

Substitute Preferred

Replace the hazardous substance, process, or equipment with something safer. Same outcome, lower risk. Replace a toxic chemical with a safer alternative. Replace a manual lift with a mechanical one.

▲
3

Isolate Strong

Separate the hazard from people. Physical barriers, guards, enclosures, LOTO, exclusion zones. The hazard still exists but people cannot reach it. LOTO (TE-IMS-SOP-HSE-003) is an isolation control.

▲
4

Engineering Controls Reliable

Design the hazard out using physical modifications. Machine guarding, ventilation systems, interlocks, noise enclosures, anti-rollover structures on vehicles. Effective because they do not rely on people remembering.

▲
5

Administrative Controls Moderate

Rules, procedures, training, signs, permits, supervision, job rotation. Effective only if people comply consistently. Includes: this JHA, the permit-to-work, toolbox talks, LOTO procedures, shift rotation for heat and fatigue management.

▲
6

Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) Last Resort

Hard hat, safety glasses, FR coveralls, safety boots, gloves, hearing protection, fall harness, respirator. PPE does not eliminate the hazard. It only reduces the injury if the hazard reaches the person. PPE must always be combined with higher-level controls for significant risks. PPE must be inspected before each use.

▼

PPE failure is common — do not rely on it alone

Gloves tear. Hard hats are not worn correctly. Respirators are not fitted. PPE fails under the exact conditions that create the hazard. If PPE is your only control for a High or Critical hazard, your JHA is not adequate. Add engineering or administrative controls above it.

Chapter 7

Worked Example:
Drilling Operations JHA

Task: Diamond core drilling — rod change and core retrieval at surface. Site: Northern Mine, Tathlith. Rig #3, Hole TT-07. Supervisor: Site Foreman. Date: 03/05/2026.

# Task Step Hazard Identified Inherent Risk Controls Applied Residual Risk
1 Signal driller to stop rotation, raise bit to safe collar height Worker in exclusion zone while rig in motion; struck by rotating rod CRITICAL Eliminate: No personnel within 3 m of rotating string. Engineering: rig interlock confirms stop before approach. Admin: verbal signal protocol. PPE: hard hat, FR coveralls. LOW
2 Apply LOTO — hydraulic and rotation power isolation Unexpected re-energization during LOTO application; hydraulic line pressurized CRITICAL Isolate: LOTO per TE-IMS-SOP-HSE-003. Bleed hydraulic lines to zero. Verify zero energy. Admin: Isolation Officer applies lock + tag. Register signed. PPE: FR coveralls, hydraulic gloves, face shield. LOW
3 Break drill rod joint at surface collar Rod slippage during breakout — hand / finger crush; joint suddenly releasing — struck-by HIGH Engineering: use pipe wrench with extension — no hand contact with thread. Admin: second person spotting only, standing clear of breakout direction. PPE: heavy leather gloves, hard hat, steel-cap boots. MEDIUM
4 Extract rod section to laydown area Drop of rod section (6 m, 25 kg) during extraction — foot / leg crush, back injury from manual handling HIGH Substitute: use rod handler / mechanical assist for all rods >15 kg. Admin: team lift plan (2 persons minimum), lift at knee height. PPE: steel-cap boots, back brace if manual. Exclusion zone: 3 m around laydown area. LOW
5 Remove inner barrel / core tube Silica-bearing dust released from core — inhalation; core tube sharp edges — lacerations HIGH Engineering: wet core retrieval where possible to suppress dust. Admin: stay upwind during extraction. PPE: P2 respirator (mandatory), safety glasses, leather gloves. No eating/drinking at core area. LOW
6 Lay core in core box and label Repetitive bending and lifting of core boxes in heat — heat exhaustion, musculoskeletal strain MEDIUM Admin: rotate core logging personnel every 60 min during summer. Hydration: 500 ml water every 30 min. Schedule heavy lifting outside peak heat (before 10:00 or after 15:00). PPE: sun hat, long sleeves, sunscreen. LOW
7 Re-run new rod string — thread and torque Pinch point between rod sections during threading; cross-threading causing sudden drop; over-torquing causing rod failure HIGH Admin: use correct torque wrench per manufacturer spec. Trained driller only to operate. Hands clear of thread area during make-up. Visual check of threads before insertion. PPE: gloves, hard hat, safety glasses. LOW
8 Remove LOTO and restart drilling Personnel still in exclusion zone when rig re-energized; LOTO removed before all workers clear CRITICAL Admin: Supervisor performs headcount and visual check — all personnel clear confirmed verbally. Isolation Officer is last to remove lock. Loudspeaker / horn signal before restart. Rig interlock confirms all-clear before start. LOW

Key observation from this example: Steps 1, 2, and 8 are CRITICAL inherent risk. All three involve rig energization / de-energization. These are the moments where most drilling fatalities occur. The controls bring all three to LOW residual risk — but only if every control is actually applied. A Supervisor who skips the LOTO verification on step 2 because "we do it every day" is one moment away from a fatality.

Chapter 8

Common mistakes
to avoid.

These are the eight errors that turn a JHA from a protective document into a piece of paper that hangs someone. Every one of these has contributed to a real injury or fatality somewhere in the industry.

01

Filling it out after the work starts

A JHA completed during or after the task is not a safety tool — it is paperwork. The whole point is to identify hazards before they are encountered. A backdated JHA is a falsified record.

✓ Fix: The JHA is the last step before the first tool is picked up. No exceptions.
02

Copying the JHA from yesterday

Conditions change. Equipment changes. Personnel change. A JHA for Hole TT-06 is not valid for Hole TT-07. Copying without review is the same as not having a JHA at all — but worse, because it creates false confidence.

✓ Fix: Re-assess every step every time. Reuse the template; not the answers.
03

Only listing "wear PPE" as a control

PPE alone is not adequate for High or Critical hazards. It is the last line of defense. A JHA that lists "safety glasses" as the control for "pressurized hydraulic line failure" has not identified a meaningful control at all.

✓ Fix: Always apply the hierarchy. PPE is always the last row, never the only row.
04

Supervisor writes it, crew just signs

A JHA completed by one person in isolation is a risk assessment for one person's knowledge. Field workers know hazards that Supervisors do not see. If the crew does not contribute, you are guaranteed to miss hazards.

✓ Fix: Ask every crew member: "What could hurt you at Step 3?" before writing anything down.
05

Not updating when conditions change

A sandstorm starts mid-shift. A piece of equipment fails. A second crew joins the work area. Each of these changes the risk profile. The original JHA no longer covers the actual task being performed.

✓ Fix: Stop work. Re-assess. Update the JHA. Re-brief and sign. Then restart.
06

Using vague task descriptions

"Drilling operations" is not a task step. "Position the rod handler arm over the collar while the bit is at safe height and LOTO is applied" is a task step. Vague steps produce vague hazard identification. Vague hazards get vague controls.

✓ Fix: Each step should describe one physical action, specific enough that anyone could read it and know exactly what to do.
07

No accountability for controls

"LOTO to be applied" is not adequate. "LOTO applied by: Isolation Officer (Ahmed Al-Rashidi) — verified by Supervisor (Khalid Mansour)" is adequate. Anonymously assigned controls are rarely performed controls.

✓ Fix: Every control gets a name. Every verification gets a second name. No exceptions.
08

Treating the JHA as paperwork, not a tool

A JHA completed to satisfy an audit but never actually used on site is a cultural failure. Supervisors who submit JHAs without conducting them are creating liability, not safety. The document is only as strong as the conversation that produced it.

✓ Fix: The quality of the JHA conversation is the quality of the safety culture. Make it real.
Chapter 9

The Five Golden Rules.

Print these. Post them on the rig. Know them by heart. If any one of these is violated, stop work immediately.

1

No JHA, No Start.

A completed and signed JHA is required before any high-risk task begins. There are no exceptions. Not for urgency. Not for "we do this every day." Not under any pressure from any person.

🚫
2

Every Person Has Stop Work Authority.

Any worker, at any level, has the unconditional right and duty to stop a task if they see an uncontrolled hazard. There is zero retaliation for exercising this right in good faith. If in doubt — stop.

✋
3

Verify Controls Before Proceeding Past Each Step.

Do not move from Step 2 to Step 3 until you have physically confirmed that every control for Step 2 is in place. Verbal confirmation is not enough for critical controls. Verify with your eyes, hands, and instruments.

✅
4

Never Sign What You Don't Understand.

Signing a JHA means: "I have read this, I understand every step and every control, and I commit to applying them." If you do not understand something, ask. If the answer does not satisfy you, do not sign. Do not start.

✍️
5

Update the JHA When Conditions Change.

A JHA written for 7 AM conditions does not cover 2 PM conditions in a Saudi summer. Whenever the task, team, equipment, environment, or scope changes, stop work and update the JHA before proceeding.
